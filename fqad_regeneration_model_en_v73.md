## Abstract
**Background:** Fluoroquinolone-Associated Disability (FQAD) is a clinically recognized but mechanistically incompletely understood multisystem lesion following fluoroquinolone exposure. Validated biomarkers, subtyping and causal therapies are lacking.**Aim:** Systematic presentation of a staged regeneration model with a clear separation between clinically acceptable care, translational hypotheses and speculative future technologies.**Method:** Narrative literature analysis of current evidence (as of March 2026). Databases: PubMed, PMC, Google Scholar, ClinicalTrials.gov, regulatory sources (FDA, EMA, BfArM). Period: 2002–2026, with a focus on post-2017 evidence. Evidence weighting: human studies > animal models > in vitro data > expert inference. In-house quantitative model calculations (e.g. chelation stoichiometry, OH• hit distribution) are marked as such and do not represent clinical measurements. If there are contradictory findings, the more conservative interpretation is preferred.**Key messages:** (1) FQAD should be understood primarily as a clinical multiorgan umbrella syndrome with several possible endophenotypes, not as a uniform pathway disease. (2) Partial regeneration up to extensive functional restoration is in principle conceivable from a cell biological perspective, but disease models, biomarkers and intervention studies are currently lacking. (3) The most realistic approach is a staged model consisting of damage stabilization, organ phenotyping, symptom- and organ-related therapy and careful re-conditioning.**Implications:** This document describes hypotheses and research directions — not a therapy recommended for use, even off-label.

## Clinical handout: FQAD brief overview for the treating physician (1 page)

**What is FQAD?**Fluoroquinolone-Associated Disability — regulatory recognized risk of long-lasting, disabling and potentially irreversible side effects following fluoroquinolone exposure by FDA (2016/2018) and EMA (2018) [9], [10]. No ICD code, no validated biomarker, no approved causal therapy. FQAD is currently a diagnosis of exclusion; The aim of the translational research (AIFM1/IDH2 fingerprint, lactate-pyruvate ratio) is to make FQAD an objectively positive diagnosis in the medium term.

**Immediate contraindications for FQAD in particular:**
- No other fluoroquinolones
- No concurrent corticosteroids (officially recognized high risk factor for tendon rupture)
- No NSAIDs promptly (GABA escalation risk, package insert warning)
- No forced stress training (risk of PEM/rhabdomyolysis if deconditioned)

**Basic diagnostics (level 1, 🟢 routine):** Blood count, CRP, B12/folate, HbA1c, TSH, ferritin, CK, creatinine/eGFR (if underweight: cystatin C), liver values, vitamin D, magnesium (whole blood), zinc, selenium, ECG. Orthostasis test for dysautonomic symptoms.

**Key laboratory values for referral to specialist center:**
- Lactate-pyruvate quotient (note collection protocol: undamaged, immediately on ice, L+P together)
- Skin biopsy (intraepidermal nerve fiber density/IENF) for mainly small fiber neuropathy
- Calprotectin + tryptase for GI symptoms and especially MCAS

**Crash protocol for acute decompensation:** Absolute stimulus protection, no diagnostic stress tests, short-term H1/H2 blockade if mast cells are suspected (cetirizine + famotidine), electrolyte balancing, no graded exercise. Monitoring until restabilization.

**Core Endophenotypes:** Musculoskeletal-tendinous | Neuropathic-dysautonomous | PEM/ME-CFS-like | Neuropsychiatric | GI-immunological (incl. MCAS/MCS) | Multisystem. Therapy and diagnostics depend on the dominant endophenotype.

## I. Safety notice

This document is a research and position paper. It does not represent a treatment protocol, does not contain any therapy recommendations and does not replace medical advice.

The off-label use of the substances discussed in this paper (including dasatinib, urolithin A, elamipretide) is associated with considerable, sometimes serious, risks; Safety and efficacy data for FQAD are lacking [1]–[4]. Every therapeutic attempt must be carried out under medical supervision and ideally as part of clinical studies.

Central nervous system drugs (SSRIs, SNRIs, gabapentinoids) can be poorly tolerated in some of those affected by FQAD; Clinically, paradoxical reactions are frequently reported [5]. If use is necessary, titration should be carried out carefully and individually. Direct FQAD-specific safety data is missing.

## II. What this model explicitly does not claim

-**No proven unit mechanics.**FQAD is not shown to be fully explained by a single mitochondrial cascade.
-**Not a validated biomarker.**No test today reliably says who is actively disease-driven, which tissue dominates or who would respond to which therapy [1], [6].
-**No approved causal therapy.**There is no approved or evidence-based recommended medication for FQAD.
-**No prediction of course.**It is unclear which patients spontaneously improve, become chronic or worsen.
-**Not equated with genetic mitochondrial diseases.**FQAD is an acquired, toxic injury - not analogous to monogenetic diseases [7], [8].
-**No off-label self-therapy.**Substances from the curative horizon (Section XIV) are not suitable for self-administration.
-**No automatic transferability**of mitochondrial medicine licenses to FQAD.
-**Do not equate biomarker improvement with clinical cure.**Whether normalized laboratory values ​​actually mean functional gain is not clear in FQAD.
-**Not “fluoride poisoning”.**FQAD is not the same as fluorosis. The covalently bound fluorine atom is not released in the body in toxic relevant quantities (see D4).

## 1.1 Regulatory and epidemiological classification (🟢 Established)

### Regulatory classification

The term FQAD was coined by the FDA in safety communications; The risk of long-lasting, disabling and potentially irreversible side effects is recognized by regulators [9]. EMA and BfArM have given comparable assessments [10]. There is no ICD code, no validated biomarker and no approved causal therapy.

### Epidemiology

The true frequency spectrum of severe long-term courses is unclear; Reliable population-based incidence data are lacking [11]. The courses range from complete recovery within weeks to years of severe disability. Factors for favorable or unfavorable outcomes are largely unknown.

**Prescription numbers and estimated side effect burden (🟢 Established):**

The AOK Scientific Institute (WIdO, 2019) [52] has extrapolated the additional risks of fluoroquinolones compared to other antibiotics based on study results: Of 3.3 million FQ-treated patients in Germany (2018), an estimated >40,000 patients suffer from additional side effects (nervous system, aorta, tendon rupture), with an estimated ~140 additional deaths/year that would not have occurred with alternative antibiotics. Extrapolated to the EU (~42 million FQ patients/year, based on EMA estimate of 350 million daily doses/year [53]), this corresponds to ~360,000 additional side effects and ~1,260 additional deaths per year. Despite BfArM application restrictions (2019) [56], FQ were the fourth most heavily prescribed antibiotic group in Germany in 2019 with ~17 million defined daily doses.

**Pharmacokinetics of Ciprofloxacin (🟢 Established):**

Maximum serum concentration is reached 1 hour after oral administration. Half-life: ~4 hours. Elimination: 40-50% unchanged in urine, 25-40% metabolized. Important: Of the identified metabolites, some have a weaker effect, but others have a stronger effect than the parent substance ciprofloxacin itself - so the toxicity does not end with the elimination of the parent substance. Toxicological data: LDLo (Human, oral): 5,714 µg/kg; LD50 (mouse, oral): 5,000 mg/kg.

**Increased risk in seniors (≥65 years):** In the EU, an estimated ~13.4 million seniors/year receive fluoroquinolones (double the prescription rate). Large cohort studies show for seniors receiving FQ therapy: tendon ruptures 2.1% (~268,000/year EU), aortic aneurysms 1.1% (~147,000/year EU), retinal detachments 0.2% (~27,000/year EU).

**EMA pharmacovigilance data (2000-2017) [54]:** Ciprofloxacin: 22,353 total cases, 1,161 deaths (most common: toxic epidermal necrolysis, fever, multi-organ failure). Levofloxacin: 30,170 total cases, 1,532 deaths (most common: pneumonia, multi-organ failure, sepsis). For both substances, tendonitis/tendon rupture are among the most commonly reported side effects - although the package insert classifies them as "very rare".
**FDA FQAD data (1997-2015) [55]:** FDA documented 178 FQAD cases with a median duration of disabling adverse events of 61.2 weeks (14 months); the longest documented duration was 9 years at the time of reporting. The proportion of disability reports among all serious side effect reports for oral antibiotics was dramatically higher for FQ than for all other antibiotics: ofloxacin 31.1%, ciprofloxacin 29.3%, levofloxacin 26.9% - compared to, for example, amoxicillin 6.0% or azithromycin 1.2%.

**Population-based incidence trends — Achilles tendon ruptures and FQ prescriptions:**

Several European countries show a temporal parallel between increasing FQ use and Achilles tendon ruptures: Finland [58]: Incidence of non-traumatic Achilles tendon ruptures increased from 2.1 (95% CI 0.3-7.7) per 100,000 person-years (1979) to 21.5 (95% CI 14.6-30.6) in 2011 — a >10-fold increase, coinciding with the broad FQ rollout. Funen County (Denmark) [59]: increase from 22 to 32 per 100,000 inhabitants (1991–2002). Malmö (Sweden) [60]: 10 cases/year (1950–1973) → 38 cases/year (1987–1991). These ecological correlations do not prove causation, but are consistent with known FQ tendon toxicity and the concomitant increase in FQ prescription numbers in the 1990s.

**Structure-activity relationships on the FQ molecule:**
The side effects of fluoroquinolones are not randomly distributed, but correlate with specific positions on the quinolone backbone [61]: Position C-6 (fluorine atom): responsible for genotoxicity and CNS toxicity. FQ are significantly more clastogenic (chromosome-breaking) than non-fluorinated quinolones. Position R7: controls GABA binding (→ seizures, anxiety) and theophylline interaction. Position C-8: controls phototoxicity (C-8F > C-8Cl > N-8 > C-8H > C-8OMe). Position R1: controls theophylline interaction and genetic toxicity. Position R5 (metal binding site): controls chelation with divalent cations (Mg²⁺, Zn²⁺, Fe²⁺) and interaction with antacids/milk.

**Bacteriophage therapy as a new FQ alternative (🟡 clinical evidence growing):**

Bacteriophages — viruses that specifically infect and lyse bacteria without harming human cells — are experiencing a renaissance as a solution to the antibiotic resistance problem [63], [64]. Clinical experience: Phage Therapy Unit Wroclaw (Poland): >3,000 patients since 2005; Eliava Institute Tbilisi (Georgia): >100,000 patients since the 1930s; national phage programs in Belgium and France since 2019.

**Key message for regulators:** With the three-way combination of conventional alternatives + bacteriophage therapy + nemonoxacin (non-fluoroquinolone),**almost 100% of the current FQ regulations**can be replaced in the future. Already today ~83% are covered by conventional AB + phages [63]. The ~17% previously considered “unreplaceable” (CAP, complicated UTI, prostatitis) can be addressed by nemonoxacin, which offers quinolone tissue penetration without the fluorine-mediated toxicity enhancement [69]. Remaining exceptions: MDR tuberculosis and febrile neutropenia in high-risk oncology patients, where FQ remains as a reserve agent pending full approval of nemonoxacin in the EU.

**Nemonoxacin (Taigexyn) — the fluorine-free quinolone as a structural FQ replacement (🟡 approved in Asia)**

Nemonoxacin is the pharmacological counter-evidence for the enabling role of fluorine (see 1.8): a non-fluoroquinolone in which the fluorine atom at C-6 has been intentionally removed. It retains the antibacterial effectiveness of the quinolone backbone (topoisomerase inhibition), but eliminates the toxicity enhancers specifically mediated by the fluorine atom:

| Property | Fluoroquinolones (Cipro/Levo) | Nemonoxacin | Clinical consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
|**Phototoxicity**| Yes (C-F cleavage by UV) | Virtually eliminated | No UV-induced radical fire |
|**Lipophilia/Ion Trapping**| High (fluorine increases membrane permeability) | Significantly reduced | Less intracellular accumulation |
|**Metabolic stability**| Very high (C-F resistant to liver degradation) | Reduced → faster degradation | Shorter tissue exposure |
|**Chelation Risk**| Yes (quinolone backbone) | Yes – residual risk remains | Mg²⁺/Zn²⁺ chelation still possible |
|**AIFM1/IDH2 binding**| Proven [2] | Not examined | Open research question |
|**Approval**| Worldwide | Taiwan (2014), China (oral + i.v.) | EU/USA: Phase III studies ongoing |

**Range of indications (approved/in studies):** Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) - here nemonoxacin shows non-inferiority to levofloxacin in phase III studies with a significantly lower side effect profile [69]. Additionally: skin/soft tissue infections (approved in China).
**Importance for FQ restriction:** Nemonoxacin could, in the long term, address the remaining ~17% of FQ indications in which specific tissue penetration and the quinolone mode of action (topoisomerase inhibition) are currently considered non-substitutable - particularly outpatient pneumonia and complicated UTIs. The combination of conventional AB + phages + nemonoxacin could in the future enable a complete FQ replacement.

## 1.2 Core Evidence: Molecular Off-Targets (🟢 Established)

### Core evidence on fluoroquinolone toxicity

>**Level of evidence:**🟢 Well supported. Human cell chemoproteomic data [2] confirmed by independent mtDNA study [3]. Pending clinical translation.

The chemoproteomic study by Reinhardt et al. (TU Munich, 2025) [2] provides the most precise molecular mapping to date: Ciprofloxacin and levofloxacin bind directly to AIFM1 and IDH2, selectively disrupt complexes I and IV of the ETC, and the resulting dysfunction activates a rescue mechanism via IDH2, which is further enhanced by simultaneous FQ inhibition. Toxicity does not occur acutely but develops over prolonged treatment duration and cellular accumulation [2], [3].

## 1.3 The role of the fluorine atom: enabler, not poison (🟢/🟡)

### D4. The role of the fluorine atom: enabler, not poison

It is often discussed in patient forums whether FQAD is "fluoride poisoning." This hypothesis is chemically untenable, but the fluorine atom plays a crucial indirect role as an enabler of toxicity.

**What is secured:**

The fluorine atom at position C6 of the quinolone ring system is covalently bound via one of the strongest bonds in organic chemistry (C-F bond energy ~450–525 kJ/mol). Under physiological conditions (37°C, pH 7.4) no significant metabolic defluorination occurs (<0.1%). Ciprofloxacin is almost completely excreted in its fluorinated form. Direct fluoride poisoning (as with inorganic sodium fluoride) is physically and chemically impossible. Fluorosis (teeth, bones/osteosclerosis) and FQAD (soft tissue, nerves) have fundamentally different symptoms and different biochemical reaction pathways [51].

**Three indirect roles of the fluorine atom in causing damage:**

| role | Mechanism | Consequence for FQAD |
|---|---|---|
|**1. Lipophilicity enhancer**| Fluorine at C6 significantly increases the lipid solubility of the molecule | Enables penetration of cell membranes and deep penetration into tendons, cartilage, CNS and mitochondria - the physical prerequisite for ion trapping (see D2) |
|**2. Liver protective shield**| The strong electron pull of the fluorine (inductive effect) electronically deactivates the quinolone ring and makes it difficult for CYP enzymes to attack | The molecule escapes liver breakdown and remains in the blood in its highly toxic 3D original form. Only this intact molecule can penetrate into enzymatic binding pockets (AIFM1, IDH2, topoisomerase) (see D3, metabolite detox). With slow metabolizers, this effect increases cumulatively. |
|**3. Chelation enhancer**| The chelation (Mg²⁺/Ca²⁺ robbery) takes place on the oxygen atoms of the keto/carboxyl group at C3/C4 - not on the fluorine itself. But the electron pull of the fluorine changes the charge distribution on this "claw" and can strengthen the bond to metal ions. | Fluorine is not the claw, but it sharpens it. The chelating ability of the entire molecule is influenced by the electronic modification of the ring [51]. |

**The central answer: Fluorine → ROS → tissue damage**

The causal pathway from the fluorine atom to mitochondrial ROS production in tenocytes (tendon cells) proceeds via a three-step detour: (1) fluorine protects the molecule from CYP degradation in the liver → the intact molecule remains in the blood; (2) Fluorine increases lipophilicity → the molecule penetrates deep into tendon tissue and accumulates in mitochondria via ion trapping; (3) there the intact, fluorinated molecule blocks the ETC via AIFM1/IDH2 [2] → electron congestion → superoxide → Fenton reaction → hydroxyl radicals (ROS) → mtDNA destruction → MMP escalation [4], [34].

The fluorine atom does not cause the damage directly as a toxic ion, but is the architectural reason why the entire molecule (a) survives liver breakdown, (b) penetrates deep enough into the tissue and (c) remains precise enough to block the mitochondrial enzymes. Without the fluorine, the older, non-fluorinated quinolones (e.g. nalidixic acid) would be significantly less permeable to tissue and less potent - and would therefore be less likely to cause serious long-term damage.

## 1.4 Clinically discussed damage axes (🟢/🟡/🔴 mixed)

### Damage axes discussed clinically and mechanistically
FQAD is currently better understood as a clinical umbrella syndrome with several possible endophenotypes [1], [6]. The following axes are supported to varying degrees:

**Well supported (primary literature + regulatory recognition):**
1. Mitochondrial dysfunction caused by oxidative stress, Top2β inhibition and direct ETC off-target effects (AIFM1, IDH2) [1]–[3]
2. Collagen damage due to upregulation of matrix metalloproteinases [13]
3. Peripheral nervous system due to oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction or direct neurotoxicity [5], [14]
4. Neuropsychiatric effects due to GABA receptor modulation and NMDA receptor overactivation [5]

**Plausible (mechanistically justified, but not systematically investigated for FQAD):**
5. Dysautonomia and small fiber neuropathy, partly with autoimmune components (GPCR autoantibodies) [15], [16]
6. Endocrine dysregulation (HPA axis) during chronic stress [17]

**Hypothetical (biologically conceivable, but FQAD-specific has hardly been investigated):**
7. Gastrointestinal-immunological axis caused by antibiotic dysbiosis, barrier disruption and mast cell activation [18]–[20]. The prevalence of clearly defined MCAS trajectories within FQAD is not known; these considerations generate hypotheses.
8. Multiple Chemical Sensitivity (MCS) as a result of the MCAS/GABA double blockade: immunological overreaction (mast cells) + neuronal misinterpretation of chemical stimuli (GABA filter failure) could explain the chemical intolerance frequently reported in FQAD. FQAD-specific not examined.

## 1.5 Chelation and structural tissue destabilization (🟡 Plausible)

### D2. Chelation and structural tissue destabilization

>**Level of evidence:**🟡 Plausible. Chelating property pharmacokinetically established (interaction with antacids/Ca/Fe/Mg). Hypothesis of independent tissue damage is biologically plausible, but has not been proven as the main FQAD-specific mechanism in isolated human studies [51].

In addition to mitochondrial toxicity, fluoroquinolones have a second, often underestimated damage mechanism: they are potent chelating agents with extremely high affinity for divalent cations, especially calcium (Ca²⁺) and magnesium (Mg²⁺) [4], [13], [51].
**Mechanism:** Biological tissues — tendons, cartilage, bones, cell membranes — are structurally stabilized by calcium bridges between proteins and matrix components. Fluoroquinolones can actively deprive these tissues of stabilizing ions (comparable to a strong complexing agent such as EDTA). This disintegration goes beyond a pure acid or radical effect - it directly attacks the physical statics of the tissue [51].

**Evidence from in vitro experiments:** Ciprofloxacin hydrochloride (the salt of a strong acid and a weak base) produces an aggressive, unbuffered environment at high local concentrations. In tissue experiments (500 mg ciprofloxacin tablet in a small amount of liquid, 8 hours exposure time) this leads to measurable dissolution of biological structures that cannot be explained by the pH value alone - control experiments with orange juice (pH ~3.5, organic acids, buffered) show no tissue decomposition at a comparable pH. The resulting clouding is not only due to insoluble excipients (cellulose, titanium dioxide, approx. 60-80% of the tablet weight), but also to real tissue dissolution due to ion removal. The crucial additional mechanism is chelation: Ciprofloxacin actively removes the structure-stabilizing cations from the tissue, which explains the differences to the pH-controlled orange juice experiment [51].

**Consequences for FQAD damage Saxony:**

**Quantitative model calculation: chelation capacity in experiments vs. in vivo**

A simplified but chemically correct example calculation for the in vitro scenario (500 mg ciprofloxacin tablet in 100 ml water, contact with muscle tissue):

| Parameters | Value |
|---|---|
| Ciprofloxacin amount | 500mg; molar mass ~331 g/mol →**~1.5 mmol**active ingredient |
| Concentration in glass (100 ml) |**15 mmol/L**(~15,000 µmol/L) |
| Mg²⁺ content muscle tissue | ~10 mmol/kg (freely available) |
| Complex stability constant (K) | ~10⁴ (log K ≈ 4.0) — strong bond |
*Law of mass action:* At K = 10,000 and a molar excess of ciprofloxacin (15 mmol/L) compared to magnesium in the tissue (~10 mmol/L), complex formation occurs almost completely. Mathematically, ciprofloxacin binds ~100% of the freely available magnesium in the direct contact area - only traces (µmol range) of free Mg²⁺ remain. The electrostatic bridges between muscle proteins (actin, myosin) and collagen break down → denaturation → the meat loses its structural strength in hours [51].

*Critical Reality Check — In-vitro vs. In-vivo:*

| Parameters | In the glass (experiment) | In the body (after 500 mg orally) |
|---|---|---|
| Ciprofloxacin concentration |**~15,000 µmol/L**|**~7.5 µmol/L**(Plasma Cmax) |
| concentration factor | Reference |**~2,000 times lower**|
| Tissue accumulation (tendon/cartilage) | — | Up to**10 times higher than plasma**(~75 µmol/L) — still ~200 times lower than in glass |

The conclusion: In the body, the drug can never remove all the magnesium from the tissue at once (otherwise a single tablet would be immediately lethal). The stoichiometry shows that for every ciprofloxacin molecule in the tendon there are at least ~133 magnesium ions. Even with immediate binding, in the absolute extreme case the drug only consumes ~0.75% of total tissue magnesium. A direct structural collapse due to mass ion theft - as in a glass of water - is physically and mathematically impossible in the human body.

**Then why does the tendon still tear? — Precision instead of mass**

The drug does not bind structural magnesium indiscriminately, but rather targets the most sensitive parts of the cell: the enzymes. Many vital enzymes (topoisomerases for DNA repair, TIMP brake systems, mitochondrial dehydrogenases) have exactly a single magnesium ion in their active center. The ciprofloxacin does not have to suck the entire cell dry - it is enough if it sits in this enzymatic keyhole and blocks this one specific ion. The comparison: Chelation effect = match (makes the cell sick), MMP enzymes = dynamite (tear down the structure).

**Ion trapping — why the drug gets stuck in cells:**
Ciprofloxacin is extremely lipophilic and penetrates cell membranes effortlessly. There are acidic compartments inside the cell (lysosomes, pH ~4.5). There the FQ molecule changes its electrical charge (protonation of the weak base) and can no longer penetrate the membrane - the ion trap snaps shut. While the plasma concentration has been falling for a long time, the drug accumulates intracellularly and reaches concentrations there that are many times the plasma level. This lysosomal storage could explain why symptoms persist or even increase for weeks to months after discontinuation [4], [51].

**Exception kidney - crystalluria:** Ciprofloxacin can actually physically crystallize in the urine at certain pH (sharp-edged needles), which mechanically injure the kidney epithelium. This is the only location in the body where physical aggregation of the active ingredient is clinically relevant.

**The chronological three-phase domino effect:**

Tissue damage in the body is a strict temporal chain reaction:

| phase | Mechanism | Result |
|---|---|---|
|**Phase 1: Chemical Overexploitation**| FQ accumulates via ion trapping in tendon/cartilage; continuously removes Mg²⁺ from enzymes and matrix proteins | Microscopically, collagen fibers lose their tension and fray; Enzymes (topoisomerases, TIMPs) lose their active center → DNA breaks, repair stops |
|**Phase 2: Biological Alarm**| Damaged collagen is recorded as an “injury”; At the same time, Mg²⁺ deficiency in mitochondria generates massive oxidative stress (ROS) | Double alarm: defective collagen + oxidative stress = cellular distress signal |
|**Phase 3: MMP Escalation**| Cells release massive amounts of MMPs (MMP-2, MMP-9, MMP-13) - biological mission: "Clean up the broken collagen" | Since FQ continues to sit in the tissue via ion trapping, the alarm signal remains permanently on "red" → MMPs then break down not only damaged but also healthy tissue at an increased rate → chronic destruction over months [13] |
*The sponge analogy:* The drug does not hit like a hammer, but rather acts like a sponge that sits deep in the cells for weeks and sucks the ions out of the building substance drop by drop. On a microscopic level, the thick collagen fibers lose their internal tension because the stabilizing cross-links are missing - they begin to fray. The tendon becomes “rotten” and loses its tensile strength without the tissue immediately visibly disintegrating as in a glass of water. It is a slow, cumulative structural impairment over weeks to months over weeks to months.

*Critical limit of chemistry:* Even if this "piece-by-piece" robbery were to continue for years, the ion withdrawal alone is not enough to cause entire muscle bundles or tendons to collapse completely. To do this, thousands of ion bridges would have to be exploded at the same time - the amount of medication in the body is stoichiometrically too low for this (see model calculation above: ~0.75%). Without the MMP enzymes, the tissue would develop microscopic cracks, but would not tear or shrink over a large area. Phase 3 (the MMP escalation) is therefore not an optional ingredient, but the mandatory prerequisite for the clinically visible structural damage.

Without the previous FQ-induced ion depletion and cell stress (phase 1+2), the MMPs would never be activated to this destructive extent. The drug provides the toxic trigger; the body carries out the destruction itself.

**What breaks immediately WITHOUT MMPs (immediate functional damage):**

Even without MMP activation, ion withdrawal causes direct damage on a functional level - the lack of individual ions in critical enzymes is enough for this:

-**DNA Destruction:** Topoisomerases often have a single Mg²⁺ in the active site; When blocked, the DNA breaks when read → cell death (apoptosis), without any MMPs [1], [3]
-**Nerve short circuit:** Nerve pathways function via ion channels (Ca²⁺, K⁺, Mg²⁺); if FQ disrupts channel regulation, nerves fire uncontrollably or die → burning sensation, neuropathy as a direct chelation/channel effect [5]
-**TIMP inhibition:** Zinc chelation by FQ additionally weakens the body's own MMP brakes (see TIMP section below) [13]

1.**Local accumulation:** Fluoroquinolones accumulate up to 10-fold higher in tendons and cartilage than in plasma, cumulatively after repeated administration over days [13], [51].
2.**The enzyme trap:** Even a small but constant Mg²⁺ withdrawal at the tissue interface can lower the activation threshold of the matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs). The MMPs then carry out the actual collagen destruction over weeks to months - the chelation effect is the igniter, the enzymes are the radical amplifier [13].

The calculation proves the destructive power in the water glass quantitatively; For FQAD pathology in the body, it is not the absolute chelation capacity that is crucial, but rather the targeted, cumulative disruption of ion-dependent cell processes in vulnerable tissue locations.

| Damage axis | Chelation mechanism | Clinical manifestation |
|---|---|---|
|**Tendons (tendinopathy/rupture)**| Withdrawal of Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ from collagen fibers → loss of cross-linking and tear strength | Tendon becomes “rotten”; Even normal stress can trigger a rupture [13] |
|**Cartilage (accelerated osteoarthritis)**| Destabilization of the cartilage matrix by ion robbery in addition to MMP activation and chondrocyte apoptosis | Increased enzymatic + structural cartilage degradation |
|**Nervous system (neuropathy)**| Disruption of ion homeostasis on nerve cell membranes; Ca²⁺ channel dysregulation | Nerve burning, hypersensitivity, small fiber damage [5] |
|**Bone (risk of osteoporosis)**| Long-term withdrawal of Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ from bone tissue | Particularly relevant for immobile patients with already increased degradation |
|**Mucous membranes**| Local necrosis with high drug concentration at the tissue interface; Breaking through the mucosal barrier | Gastrointestinal erosions, local tissue damage [51] |

**Magnesium as a protective factor - extended reason:** Since ciprofloxacin, as a chelating agent, actively "searches" for ions, if the magnesium level is low, the drug will obtain its binding partners directly from the tissue (bones, tendons, cartilage, nerve membranes). Sufficient magnesium supply is therefore not just a general cofactor, but rather a concrete protection against the ion-robbing effect of the active ingredient - the supply of magnesium before and during each fluoroquinolone administration is biologically based and is mentioned in specialist information as an indication of interaction [4], [51].
**Evidence status:** The chelating property of fluoroquinolones is pharmacokinetically established (hence the known interaction with antacids, calcium, iron, magnesium). The hypothesis that this mechanism makes an independent contribution to tissue damage beyond the reduction in bioavailability is biologically plausible, but has not been proven as the main FQAD-specific mechanism in isolated human studies [51].

**Chelate interactions: bioavailability reduction through simultaneous ion supply**

The chelating affinity of fluoroquinolones is so strong that when taken orally with certain ion-containing substances, it reduces the absorption of the active ingredient in the gastrointestinal tract by up to 90%. These pharmacokinetically established data provide quantitative evidence of the strength of the chelation effect (values refer primarily to ciprofloxacin; minimally weaker for levofloxacin/moxifloxacin, but highly clinically relevant):

| Substance class | Examples | Reduction of bioavailability |
|---|---|---|
| Antacids (Al³⁺/Mg²⁺) | Maaloxan, Talcid, Rennie, Gaviscon | up to 85-90% |
| Iron preparations (Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺) | Ferrosanol, iron tablets | up to 80-90% |
| Zinc supplements | Zinc tablets, zinc histidine | approx. 50-70% |
| Calcium preparations (high doses) | Calcium effervescent tablets (500-1000 mg) | approx. 40-60% |
| Milk and dairy products | Cow's milk (from ~300 ml), yoghurt, quark | approx. 30–50% (up to 70% if consumed at high levels) |
| Multivitamins | A-Z tablets with mineral mix | approx. 30-50% |
| Magnesium supplements | Magnesium citrate, oxide | approx. 30-50% |

*Time window:* These extreme reductions only occur when they are present in the stomach. After 2 hours in the body, the blockage effect decreases drastically. Specialist information therefore recommends: Take FQ at least 2 hours before or 4-6 hours after substances containing ions.

*Double reading of this data:* The same chelation affinity that blocks absorption in the intestine acts as an ion robber of enzymes and collagen after absorption in the tissue (see D5, front 0). The table thus indirectly quantifies the strength of the chelation effect that drives tissue damage after systemic distribution.

*Emergency Note:* In the event of an acute accidental overdose, strong binding antacids or iron preparations physically represent the strongest blockage of absorption. However, they never replace immediate contact with a poison control center or emergency medical care.
**Scientific classification of the in vitro experiment:**

The experiment did not provide toxicological research with any new insights into the mechanism of action. Chelation, MMP upregulation and collagen damage caused by fluoroquinolones have been demonstrated in cell culture studies and animal experiments for decades [13], [51]. A toxicologist would classify the experiment as "non-physiological": the flesh decays due to the sheer concentration of the active ingredient (brute chemical violence), while tissue destruction in the body occurs via the biological domino effect (mitochondria blockade → oxidative stress → MMP activation). The glass shows the correct end result (rotten tissue), but via a different route than in the living organism.

The true value is didactic: the experiment makes the invisible visible. It's physical evidence of the molecule's raw reactivity, helping sufferers contextualize the class of compounds to skeptical doctors — not a scientific breakthrough, but a powerful visual aid.

**Why the stomach survives the pill — three protective mechanisms:**

The obvious question as to whether the tablet “breaks down” the stomach in the same way as the meat in the glass is legitimate, but the answer is reassuring:

1.**Mucous membrane armor:** The stomach is built to handle extremely harsh chemicals. Stomach acid (pH 1.5-3) is far more corrosive than ciprofloxacin. A thick layer of mucosa with basic bicarbonate protects the epithelium - the tablet hits this protective armor, not naked tissue.
2.**Dilution and transit:** Ingestion with 200-300 ml of water, dilution with gastric juices, mixing through peristalsis and transit into the small intestine within 1-2 hours - the hour-long point extreme concentration of the glass experiment never occurs.
3.**Enrichment occurs elsewhere:** FQs accumulate not in the stomach, but in tendons, cartilage, macrophages and CNS [13]. The tissue damage there is not "chemical melting from outside", but rather the gradual biological domino effect (Mito blockade → Mg²⁺ withdrawal → MMP activation over weeks to months).

What the fluoroquinolone actually destroys in the GI tract is not the stomach wall, but the microbiome - with the consequences (dysbiosis → barrier disorder → MCAS), which are described in detail in the GI-immunological axis (organ axis 3).

**TIMPs — the natural brakes against MMP destruction:**
In order to inhibit the out-of-control matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs), the body produces natural antagonists: TIMPs (Tissue Inhibitors of Metalloproteinases). These proteins act as brake blocks around the catalytic centers of the MMPs and stop the enzymatic breakdown of collagen. For TIMP synthesis, the body absolutely needs zinc (Zn²⁺) as a cofactor - since fluoroquinolones also chelate zinc, an FQ-induced zinc deficiency can further weaken the body's own MMP brake. As a cofactor of glutathione peroxidase, selenium is indirectly involved in protection against oxidative tissue degradation [13], [34].

Consequence for supplementation: Zinc and selenium status should be tested in a laboratory and, if there is a deficiency, specifically compensated for - no blind high-dose supplementation, but diagnostics → compensation → monitoring (analogous to the magnesium strategy).

**The Supplementation Paradox: The Same Ions as Saviors and Radical Amplifiers**

The logic “the drug robs magnesium/zinc/calcium/iron, so you have to give them in large quantities” is convincing, but it is a biochemical minefield when FQAD is present. What prevents absorption in the stomach as a chelate blocker (see table above) can either start the healing process inside the damaged cell or act as a radical amplifier:

| ion | Therapeutic potential | Risk at FQAD | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|
|**Magnesium (Mg²⁺)**| Wichtigster Baustein: füllt den zellulären „Mörtel" in Enzymen und Kollagen wieder auf; As an NMDA antagonist, blocks the toxic Ca²⁺ influx [4], [51] | *Transport problem:* Damaged cells often have blocked or lost ion channels → Mg²⁺ floats in the blood, but has difficulty getting into the starved cell. Requires months of patience and bioavailable forms (glycinate, malate, threonate) | Priority 1. Laboratory-guided (whole blood analysis), dose up slowly, prefer bioavailable forms. No “a lot helps a lot” |
|**Zinc (Zn²⁺) + Manganese (Mn²⁺)**| "Ignition key" for SOD (superoxide dismutase, the most important cellular fire extinguisher) and TIMPs (MMP brakes) [13], [34] | In case of overdose: copper depletion, GI irritation, paradoxical immunosuppression | Laboratory-guided testing and correcting if there is a deficiency. Not as high-dose monotherapy |
|**Iron (Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺)**| Essential for severe anemia; Blocks FQ absorption most strongly in the stomach (80-90%) |**Absolute radical amplifier in active oxidative stress.**Free iron catalyzes the Fenton reaction: Fe²⁺ + H₂O₂ → OH• (hydroxyl radicals). As long as the mitochondrial fire smolders, any unnecessary iron intake significantly increases radical production [4], [34] |**Only in cases of laboratory-confirmed anemia and under medical supervision.**Handle restrictively in the acute phase of FQAD. Check ferritin and transferrin saturation |
|**Calcium (Ca²⁺)**| Bone protection in the event of immobility; Collagen cofactor |**Neurological risk:** FQ block GABA receptors and overstimulate NMDA receptors → uncontrolled Ca²⁺ influx into nerve cells (excitotoxicity) → nerve death, burning, cramps. Moderate dietary intake; Mg²⁺ as an NMDA opponent has priority |

**Tiered supplementation protocol (clinically pragmatic, not FQAD validated):**

1.**Acute phase (damage stabilization):** Top up magnesium (priority 1) + check zinc/selenium and compensate if there is a deficiency. Iron and calcium restrictive (only in laboratory-confirmed deficiency). No blind polysupplementation.
2.**Stabilization phase:** Further optimize magnesium and zinc in the laboratory. Iron only after the acute oxidative stress has subsided (clinically: no more active crashes, more stable exercise tolerance) and only if anemia is proven.
3.**Re-conditioning:** Calcium and Vitamin D/K2 for bone protection, especially in cases of immobility. Always accompanied by sufficient magnesium as an NMDA antagonist.

The basic rule remains: diagnostics → targeted compensation → monitoring. “A lot helps a lot” doesn’t work with FQAD — the system is so unstable that precision wins over dosage.

**The Glycine Paradox: Why the Carrier May Be More Toxic Than the Mineral**

>**Level of evidence:**🟡 Plausible. Glycine as an NMDA co-agonist is neurochemically proven; The specific hypersensitivity in FQAD has been clinically reported but not systematically investigated.
A common clinical conundrum: FQAD sufferers report massive fatigue after as little as 20 mg of magnesium glycinate — a dose that is pharmacologically almost negligible (~2-3 mg elemental Mg, ~17 mg glycine). The standard answer, "Take more magnesium," makes the problem worse. The explanation lies not in the magnesium, but in the carrier molecule:

| Problem | Mechanism | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
|**Glycine = NMDA co-agonist**| Glycine is an obligate co-agonist on the GluN1 subunit of the NMDA receptor. If the nervous system is intact, this is harmless because GABA functions as a counterbalance [5] | With FQ-blocked GABA receptors, the brake is missing → just 17 mg of glycine can trigger a neuro-inflammatory cascade: NMDA overstimulation → excitotoxicity → the broken mitochondria cannot supply the energy → system crashes → leaden exhaustion |
|**Reaction is qualitative, not quantitative**| The problem is not “too little magnesium”, but rather the intolerance of the carrier substance when the nervous system is damaged More magnesium glycinate = more glycine = more NMDA stimulation = worse fatigue. The assumption that it is a simple deficiency that can be remedied by higher dosage is untenable |

**MCAS-safe forms of magnesium (glycine-free):**

| shape | Why safer with FQAD/MCAS | Cave |
|---|---|---|
|**Magnesium malate**| Malate goes directly into the citrate cycle → supports ATP production instead of stimulating NMDA | For citrate sensitivity (Aspergillus fermentation) as an alternative to Mg citrate |
|**Magnesium Taurate**| Taurine has a GABAergic (calming) effect instead of excitatory → counterbalance to FQ-GABA blockade | For very severe MCAS, test individually |
|**Magnesium oxide**(low dose) | Simplest form, no bioactive ligand; low bioavailability = slow, gentle absorption | May cause diarrhea at higher doses |
|**Transdermal MgCl₂**| Completely bypasses the GI tract and nervous system (see foot bath protocol) | Dilute brine, only soles of feet (see paradoxical weakness reaction) |

**Molecular Mimicry: Functional Vitamin B6 Blockade as a Bridge Between Nervous System and Mast Cells (🟡 Plausible)**

> **Evidence level:** 🟡 The structural similarity of fluoroquinolones to vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) is chemically established; the functional consequence (receptor blockade rather than true deficiency) is mechanistically plausible and clinically consistent, but not investigated in controlled FQAD-specific studies [77].

Fluoroquinolones possess heterocyclic ring structures that are structurally similar to the vitamin B6 molecule (pyridoxine). Through this "doppelganger problem" (molecular mimicry), FQ molecules can occupy the binding sites (lock-and-key principle) of B6-dependent enzymes and displace the real vitamin B6 — even though standard lab values show normal or even elevated B6 levels in the blood. This is therefore not a classic deficiency but a **functional blockade at the receptor** [77].

**Why this is catastrophic in FQAD — two systems fail simultaneously:**

| Blocked system | Mechanism | Clinical consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **GABA production** | Vitamin B6 (active form: P5P) is an obligate cofactor of glutamate decarboxylase (GAD), which converts glutamate (excitatory) to GABA (inhibitory). With B6 blockade, GABA synthesis fails [5], [77] | Nerves fire uncontrollably → dysautonomia, panic attacks, insomnia, muscle twitching. This additively amplifies the known direct GABA-A receptor blockade by FQ (see 1.4) |
| **DAO production (histamine degradation)** | B6/P5P is the most important cofactor of diaminoxidase (DAO), which degrades histamine in the gut. With B6 blockade, DAO activity collapses [77] | Histamine accumulates uncontrollably → mast cell escalation (MCAS), immediate reactions to foods. ## 1.6 Ciprofloxacin vs. Levofloxacin (🟡 Plausible)

### D3. Ciprofloxacin vs. Levofloxacin: Structural differences and clinical consequences
Both molecules possess the same toxic fluoroquinolone double ring (the “toxicophoric core”) that is responsible for ETC blockade, chelation, and MMP escalation [2]. The Reinhardt study [2] shows that both fit into the binding pockets of AIFM1 and IDH2. The 3D differences mainly affect the side arms and determine the disposal and accumulation dynamics:

| Property | Ciprofloxacin | Levofloxacin |
|---|---|---|
| Structural feature | Cyclopropyl group (flat, compact) | Additional oxazine ring; L-enantiomer (asymmetric, “sharpened”) |
| Main clearance | Dual: liver (CYP1A2) + kidney | Almost 100% kidney; Liver hardly plays a role |
| Plasma half-life | ~4 h (with normal clearance) | ~6–8 h (longer circulation time) |
| Primary eye of the needle | CYP1A2 capacity (liver genetics) | Creatinine clearance/eGFR (renal function) |
| Ion trapping risk | Increased in slow detoxifiers (slow metabolizers). Increased in cases of impaired kidney function or dehydration; longer HWZ = more time for tissue accumulation |

**Why Levofloxacin Often Causes More Severe Damage — Three Converging Factors:**

1.**Lack of “back door”:** Ciprofloxacin has two pathways for degradation (liver + kidney); If one fails, the other can partially compensate. Levofloxacin only affects the kidneys — if you have even slightly impaired eGFR, dehydration or age, there is no Plan B.
2.**Longer exposure time:** The half-life, which is almost twice as long, gives the molecule more time to penetrate tissue with poor blood circulation (tendons, cartilage, CNS) and to be stored there accumulatively via ion trapping.
3.**Perfected 3D key:** As a pure L-enantiomer, Levofloxacin is the sharpened version - all ineffective molecular parts have been removed, which maximizes the binding affinity to human enzymes (AIFM1, IDH2, topoisomerase).

**Metabolite detoxification — how the liver destroys the key:**

When ciprofloxacin encounters CYP enzymes in the liver, the 3D shape of the molecule is changed (side chains broken off, sulfate/oxo groups stuck on → sulfociprofloxacin, oxociprofloxacin). These metabolites have lost their perfect 3D fit for the enzymatic binding pockets — they no longer fit into the "vault rooms" of AIFM1 or topoisomerase II and can no longer chelate there. The metabolites are pharmacological “junk”: water-soluble, renally excretable, no longer capable of being used as weapons [51].
The FQAD problem is therefore not toxic liver degradation, but rather a lack of liver degradation: With genetic slow detoxifiers (slow metabolizers), the intact, highly toxic original molecule remains in the blood and has time to penetrate tissue via ion trapping.

**Coffee sensitivity as a clinical CYP1A2 indicator:**

Caffeine is broken down by CYP1A2 — the same enzyme that metabolizes ciprofloxacin (and which further inhibits ciprofloxacin). Patients who respond to a cup of coffee with heart palpitations that last for hours, insomnia, or extreme restlessness are most likely CYP1A2 slow detoxifiers. This coffee sensitivity is a simple, free clinical indication of increased risk of ciprofloxacin accumulation. This analogy does not apply to levofloxacin (no CYP1A2 dependence), where kidney function is the crucial bottleneck.

**Related Drug Classes — Cross Vulnerability:**

| Drug class | Structural/functional analogy to FQ | Clinical Consequence for FQAD Risk Assessment |
|---|---|---|
|**Malaria drugs (quinolines):** Mefloquine, Chloroquine, Hydroxychloroquine | Same quinoline ring as chemical skeleton. Mefloquine: FDA black box warning for long-lasting, partly irreversible neurotoxicity | Patients with severe reactions to malaria prophylaxis/hydroxychloroquine often have a genetic vulnerability to this ring structure → extreme caution with FQ |
|**Topoisomerase inhibitors (chemotherapy):** Etoposide, Doxorubicin | Attack the same cellular target (topoisomerase II) as FQ — intentionally rather than “accidentally.” “Chemo-Brain” and neuropathy due to identical NAD⁺/ATP collapse | Patients after chemotherapy have no DNA repair regeneration reserve for new chemically induced DNA breaks |
|**CYP Detox Twins:** Codeine (CYP2D6), Antidepressants (CYP2D6), Isoniazid (NAT2) | Identical detoxification enzymes as for ciprofloxacin. Hypersensitivity to these substances = indication of slow metabolizer genetics | Extreme codeine sensitivity, increased SSRI effect or coffee sensitivity = clinical warning signal for increased risk of FQ accumulation |
**Underweight and creatinine trap:** In patients with very low muscle mass (e.g. <50 kg), serum creatinine can life-threateningly overestimate kidney function. Creatinine is formed from muscle creatine - little muscle mass = little creatinine = falsely "perfect" eGFR values. The doctor does not adjust the levofloxacin dose downward, and the toxic burden on the little remaining tissue is massively excessive. If you are underweight, kidney function should be determined using cystatin C (independent of muscle mass) instead of creatinine.

## 1.7 Full Reaction Cascade: 6 Fronts (🟡 Plausible)

### D5. Complete reaction cascade: From molecule to tissue damage

>**Level of evidence:**🟡 Individual mechanisms supported experimentally [1]–[4], [13], [34], [51]. The following six fronts describe the chronological causal chain from chelation to the radical explosion to the final damage. Fronts 0-2 are triggers; Fronts 3–5 are the consequential damage caused by OH• radicals, whose distribution to cellular targets follows the competition kinetics. Evidence status: Individual mechanisms supported experimentally [1]–[4], [13], [34], [51]; the combination to form a linear cascade is a pathobiochemically plausible working hypothesis.

#### The triggers

**Front 0 — Enzymatic disarmament and loss of structure (chelation):**

(1) Intact, lipophilic FQ penetrates tendons/cartilage/intracellular space → (2) acts as a strong chelating agent → (3) local withdrawal of Mg²⁺, Mn²⁺, Zn²⁺, Ca²⁺ → (4) cellular “fire extinguisher” (superoxide dismutase/SOD, Mn²⁺-dependent) and DNA repair enzymes (polymerases, Mg²⁺-dependent) lose their catalytic metal ion and fail → (5) Collagen fibrils lose stabilizing Mg²⁺ "mortar" (mechanical destabilization) → (6) FQ anchors itself permanently in the tissue through the bound ion (ion trapping, see D2) [4], [13], [51].

**Front 1 - Metabolic short circuit (dark reaction in deep tissue):**
(1) Intact FQ blocks topoisomerase / ETC complexes I+IV [2] → (2) electron accumulation on the respiratory chain → (3) premature O₂ reduction → (4) superoxide (O₂•⁻) is formed → (5) SOD converts to H₂O₂ (but SOD is already weakened by front 0) → (6) Fenton reaction on free iron → mass emission of hydroxyl radicals (OH•) [4], [34].

**Front 2 — UV phototoxicity (light reaction in skin/eyes):**

Only relevant in exposed skin, not active in deep tissue: (1) FQ absorbs UVA photons → (2) molecule jumps to excited triplet state → (3) energy transfer to O₂ OR photolytic breakage of the C-F bond → (4) massive formation of singlet oxygen (¹O₂), FQ radicals and local free fluoride (F⁻).**Here - and only here - the drug actually decomposes itself and damages the tissue with its own debris. This is the only context in which true fluoride release occurs**— quantitatively small compared to the overall damage, but relevant locally in the skin for photodermatitis and photosensitivity.

As soon as OH• is created by front 1 and the protective shields by front 0 are missing, the competition kinetics takes effect - the radical reacts within nanoseconds with the next accessible biomolecule:

|---|---|---|---|---|
|**3. ## 1.10 Metabolic emergency power (🟡 Plausible)

### Metabolic emergency power: Why standard labs hide the damage

In severe mitochondrial dysfunction, the cell switches from aerobic energy production (36 ATP per glucose molecule via the respiratory chain) to anaerobic glycolysis (2 ATP per molecule, end product: lactate) [4], [35]. This "emergency power generator" explains several clinical phenomena:

**Why standard blood counts look normal:** At rest, the liver eliminates lactate produced via the Cori cycle quickly enough to keep blood counts within the norm. Standard laboratory parameters (cholesterol, liver enzymes, blood sugar) measure homeostasis outcomes, not the cellular price thereof [35].

**Why minimal stress leads to collapse (PEM biochemistry):** During stress, energy requirements increase; Glycolysis, which is 18 times more inefficient, suddenly produces more lactate than the liver can eliminate. Local tissue acidification causes muscle heaviness, pain, and cognitive decline—the precise biochemistry of post-exertion malaise [23], [35].
**Consequence for diagnostics:** An isolated lactate value at rest is not sufficient; The lactate-pyruvate quotient is crucial (see stage 2, phase B), as it shows the backlog in front of the blocked mitochondria. In addition, blood collection must be carried out in strict protocol (see biomarker protocol below) [35].

## 1.11 Chronification — four hypotheses (🟡/🔴)

### Chronification — four hypotheses

>**Level of evidence:**🟡 Plausible for hypotheses 1-3 (mtDNA persistence, ion trapping, epigenetics). 🔴 Hypothetical for Hypothesis 4 (cumulative damage accumulation upon re-exposure). Longitudinal clinical data are completely lacking.

Why symptoms persist for years remains a central open question [4]:

1. Prolonged oxidative stress destroys mtDNA; faulty cytochrome complexes create permanent electron leakage
2. FQ protein and FQ cation complexes persist in cells for years - the ion trapping mechanism (see D2) explains the intracellular accumulation and the gradual "bit by bit" ion withdrawal over weeks to months after discontinuation
3. Epigenetic changes in gene regulation become permanent after FQ exposure
4.**Cumulative Damage from Repeated Exposure**— each FQ dose leaves a microscopic footprint; Repeated use escalates the risk exponentially

#### Cumulative damage: Why the second or third dose often triggers the crash

The fact that not every first tablet leads to an immediate collapse, but repeated intake (months or years later) triggers the severe FQAD syndrome, is explained by four converging accumulation mechanisms:

| Mechanism | What happens | Why the next dose is more dangerous |
|---|---|---|
|**Cumulative Ion Trapping**| FQ accumulation in tendons/cartilage is cumulative with repeated administration [13], [51] | Each new intake replenishes the cellular depots more deeply; the chronic Mg²⁺ withdrawal becomes more massive with each round |
|**Irreversible mtDNA scars**| Unlike nuclear DNA, mitochondrial DNA has hardly any repair mechanisms. Oxidative damage (Fenton reaction) from the first dose often remains permanent [3], [4] | During re-exposure, the FQ encounters already damaged mitochondria without a compensation reserve → immediate crash into anaerobic emergency power |
|**Used regeneration reserve**| Intracellular Mg²⁺ and Zn²⁺ stores (especially in tendons/cartilage) take months to years to replenish | If re-exposure occurs before complete regeneration, the chelation effect continues unabated on enzymes and collagen
|**Senescence accumulation**| Each FQ administration drives further healthy cells into the irreversible “senescent state” (SASP secretion, production stop) via DNA breaks → p53 → senescence [34] | At some point the ratio tips over: too many SASP-secreting senescent cells, too few functional fibroblasts → chronic tissue breakdown without replenishment |

The body does not forget an FQ dose. The first dose is often compensated for by the healthy cells working overtime. The repeated dose is the straw that breaks the camel's back, already full and weakened.

## 1.12 Why courses are so different - individual vulnerability

### Lack of positions in science

**A. Lack of disease model.**FQAD is clinically recognized but not as a deciphered mechanism. Probably an umbrella of endophenotypes: tendon-dominant, neuro-dominant, dysautonomous, nephrologic, mitochondrial-metabolic, GI/MCAS-dominant, ME/CFS-like [1], [6].

**B. Missing biomarkers.**No test today says who is actively disease-driven or would respond to which therapy [6], [32].

**C. Missing intervention studies.**For elementary questions: Which stress helps? Which diet? Which supportive pharmacology?

**D. Missing regeneration endpoints.**The bridge between biomarker and clinical benefit is not built.

**E. Missing failure data.**Publication bias — e.g. B. the failure of EPI-743 in mitochondrial diseases is rarely discussed [33].

**F. Lack of explanation for course variability.**Why some sufferers recover and others become chronic is perhaps the most pressing unanswered question.

### Why courses are so different - known and suspected factors
The fact that two people receive the same dose of the same fluoroquinolone and experience completely different outcomes is medically frustrating, but not illogical. There is no gold standard biomarker that predicts who will become chronic [6]. The basic biochemical fact: The drug chemically does the same thing in all people — it blocks topoisomerase, binds to AIFM1/IDH2 and performs chelation. The difference is not due to a different pill, but rather due to individual armor and repair capacity.

**Seven Levels of Individual Vulnerability — Affected vs. Non-Affected:**

| level | For those not affected | For those affected | Testability |
|---|---|---|---|
|**1. AIFM1/IDH2 fit**| FQ slips into the binding pocket, binding is loose → enzyme throws off the molecule, respiratory chain quickly normalizes [2] | Genetic variants (SNPs) could create a perfect 3D fit → FQ hooks, blocks ETC extremely long → massive electron jam → ROS explosion | 🔴 Research: Targeted NGS + DiffDock Simulation (Level 4-5) |

In those who are not affected, there is a short-term cellular stress that the system repairs effortlessly. In those affected, the drug encounters a combination of slower clearance, precisely fitting enzyme pockets, overwhelmed repair mechanisms and depleted mineral stores. The initial spark triggers a cumulative domino effect (DNA breaks → senescence → Treg collapse → MCAS).

The following table summarizes the other factors:

| factor | Level of evidence | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
|**Dominant endophenotype**| Plausible | Not everyone has the same damage type. Tendinous, neuropathic, dysautonomic and nephrological damage have very different chances of regeneration. |

|**Kidney Function / Clearance**| Well supported | Same tablet dose ≠ same internal exposure. Differences in eGFR, hydration and concomitant medication massively alter tissue accumulation. Dosage is clinically based on creatinine clearance. |

|**Secondary Chronification Drivers**| Clinically known | After the initial biological damage, sleep loss, deconditioning, PEM/crash patterns, deficiency states, protective posture and fear of stress intensify the progression - not because the illness is "psychological", but because multiple amplifiers accumulate. |
|**Regeneration reserve**| Clinically plausible | Initial fitness, sleep quality, metabolic health, previous illnesses, cortisone exposure and type of tissue affected determine whether recovery or chronification occurs. |

**Important:** If an acquaintance got better with high-dose vitamins, that does not prove causality. The course can be due to milder damage, other organs affected or greater regeneration reserve - not to a specific supplement. There is no reliable evidence of causal FQAD healing for high-dose vitamins.

The cleanest medical formulation: "Some affected people recover because their damage is less, more reversible or limited to better-regenerating systems; others become chronic due to less favorable risk factors, greater organ involvement or secondary chronification mechanisms. This is multifactorial and cannot be explained by a single trick."

**Lactate-pyruvate protocol (critical for validity):**

An isolated lactate value at rest is often inconspicuous in mitochondrial dysfunction because the liver eliminates lactate quickly enough at rest via the Cori cycle [35]. Therefore:

1.**Uncongested blood collection**— no tourniquet or minimal congestion (tourniquet creates local ischemia → lactate artifact)
2.**Immediately on ice**— Centrifuge tube or separate plasma within 15 minutes (lactate is extremely volatile; room temperature will distort the value)
3.**Measure lactate AND pyruvate**- only the quotient (L/P ratio) shows the backlog in front of the mitochondria; isolated lactate often obscures the defect
4.**Ideal case: two-point measurement**- once at rest, once after a defined submaximal load (e.g. 3 minutes of climbing stairs, only if freedom from PEM is documented). The stress measurement shows the collapse of hepatic lactate clearance, which is not visible in the resting value

Note: Many general practitioners' practices and standard laboratories do not adhere to this protocol (lack of immediate cooling, lack of pyruvate determination). Results from non-protocol-compliant testing are of no diagnostic value.

In terms of statutory health insurance: cortisol daily profile, zonulin, skin punch biopsy, GPCR autoantibodies, M2-PK, GSH/GSSG, 8-OHdG, nitrotyrosine and comparable parameters are often not reimbursed or only reimbursed to a limited extent in everyday statutory health insurance (GKV, Germany/Austria). Patients should be transparently informed about possible out-of-pocket costs.

#### Urine diagnostics: What standard urine tests show and hide

Urine offers three diagnostic levels:

**Basic coverage (Level 1):** Urine status with albumin/creatinine ratio excludes primary kidney disease and significant proteinuria. Glucose and ketone tests rule out metabolic imbalance due to diabetes. Exclusion of active urinary tract infections (since FQAD symptoms often occur after infection treatment).
**Special marker (level 2-3):**8-OHdG in 24-hour urine collection shows oxidative mtDNA destruction [34]. Amino acid profile and nitrogen excretion can objectify catabolic tissue degradation. Methylhistamine in urine in particular due to mast cell activation.

**Practical tip - Morning urine pH profile:** A morning urine pH profile over 3-5 days (with test strips, self-measurement) can be additionally informative: If the pH remains firmly below 6.0 even after an alkaline diet, this indicates an overload of the body's own buffer systems. A healthy fluctuating pH (acid in the morning, alkaline after meals) speaks against severe systemic acid-base dysregulation. Evidence status: clinical-pragmatic, not FQAD-validated.

#### Diagnostic Chain of Evidence: Causality Approach

There is no single test that “proves” with certainty that the fluoroquinolone alone is the cause. FQAD remains a diagnosis of exclusion [9], [10]. However, the following combination can objectify the typical damage fingerprint:

| stage | test | Shows | Evidence class |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Exposure | Anamnesis: FQ intake, temporal context | Plausible chain of causality | Clinical |
| 2. Loss of function | Lactate-pyruvate quotient (stress protocol) | Shift to anaerobic emergency power | Special diagnostics |
| 3. Structural damage | Skin punch biopsy (IENF density) | Small fiber neuropathy | Special Center |
| 4. Molecular fingerprint | 8-OHdG, possibly AIFM1/IDH2 functional analysis [2] | Oxidative mtDNA damage, ETC blockade | Research-related |
| 5. Exclusion | Standard Laboratory (Level 1) | No competing explanation | Routine |

The combination of exposure + loss of function + structural evidence + exclusion constitutes the strongest clinically available causality approximation. The molecular fingerprint (AIFM1/IDH2) is currently only available in research contexts [2].

#### Level 3 — Research markers (only for studies/centers, not clinical routine)

31P magnetic resonance spectroscopy (ATP in muscle), PBMC respirometry, circulating cell-free mtDNA, acylcarnitine profiles. These markers are important to the research agenda but are not currently part of clinical care.

**Calculation of remaining mitochondrial capacity from PBMC laboratory values (🟡 special diagnostics)**

>**Level of evidence:**🟡 The individual parameters are established in laboratory chemistry; merging them into a capacity score is a quantitative working hypothesis, not a validated diagnostic index.
The "mitochondria baseline profile" (PBMC, available through specialized laboratories such as MMD Magdeburg) provides six parameters that can be put into a mathematical relationship in FQAD to estimate the remaining cellular energy.**Cave:** Standard laboratory findings often interpret these values ​​for healthy populations — in the case of toxic FQ damage, identical numbers can have a completely different meaning.

| Parameters | What he shows | FQAD Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
|**mtDNA:ntDNA ratio**(Reference: ~400 ± 230) | Mitochondrial “factory size” — number of mitochondria per cell | High value (e.g. >630) = compensatory mitochondrial proliferation. The body has accumulated masses of power plants to compensate for the failure of any one — a sign of chronic dysfunction, not health
|**Mitochondrial ATP**(pmol/10⁵ PBMC) | Actual energy production at rest | Must be interpreted in relation to the mtDNA:ntDNA ratio. “Normal” ATP with double the number of mitochondria = every single power plant runs at half power
|**PGC-1α expression**(rel. to GAPDH) | Master switch for mitochondrial regeneration (biogenesis) | Hardly detectable (e.g. 0.00031) = lack of new formation. Defective mitochondria accumulate in the cell and are not replaced by fresh ones - exactly the PGC-1α biogenesis trap of the cascade model (1.9, phase 3) |
|**Nrf-2 expression**(rel. to GAPDH) | Master switch of antioxidant defense | Slightly increased = oxidative stress is present, but the immune system is overwhelmed. Low/normal with high mitochondrial number = mitochondria hardly run (emergency power), therefore little electron leakage - not a sign of health, but of a blocked respiratory chain |
|**Rhodanase expression**(rel. to GAPDH) | Formation of iron-sulfur clusters for ETC complexes I, II, III | Very low (e.g. 0.0072) = almost no Fe-S cluster formation. These clusters are the essential “power cables” of the respiratory chain. Without it, aerobic energy production (36 ATP/glucose) cannot physically take place → cell is forced into anaerobic emergency power when stressed (2 ATP/glucose) |
|**mt4977 deletion mutant**| Most common “aging deletion” of mtDNA | Undetectable = no gross structural mtDNA destruction. Caveat: More subtle damage (point mutations, oxidative base modifications such as 8-oxo-dG) is not recorded |

**Three-step capacity calculation (hypothetical, illustrative):**
*Step 1 — Efficiency Quotient (ATP per Mitochondrion):*
If mtDNA:ntDNA is ~210% of the reference value (twice the factory size) but ATP is considered "low" relative to this → the individual mitochondrion produces**<50% of normal capacity**at rest. The body compensates through mass, not efficiency.

*Step 2 — Load Capacity (Rhodanase Bottleneck):*
Almost no Fe-S cluster formation → Respiratory chain physically blocked during exercise → Cell has to switch to glycolysis (2 instead of 36 ATP) →**Energy yield under exercise: ~5–6% of maximum aerobic capacity**. This explains why minimal exertion (sitting up in bed, digesting food) immediately leads to PEM crashes.

*Step 3 — Regenerative Ability (PGC-1α Block):*
Hardly detectable PGC-1α = no new formation of mitochondria → the system cannot repair itself → chronic accumulation of dysfunctional power plants. This is molecular evidence for the “biogenesis trap” of the cascade model.

**Clinical consequence:** The standard laboratory finding (“low ATP production indicates high efficiency”) is a misinterpretation in the case of toxic FQ damage. The combination of increased mitochondrial mass + low ATP/mitochondrion + missing rhodanase + blocked PGC-1α shows a system that survives on low power in the resting state, but immediately enters energetic collapse under any stress. This constellation of findings should be presented to the treating physician as objective, laboratory evidence of the mitochondrial basis of exercise intolerance.

#### Stage 4 — Personalized bioinformatic risk analysis (🔴 Research vision)

>**Note:** The following concepts are relevant only to research groups, not to clinical decisions or personal risk assessment. They cannot diagnose FQAD or guide treatment.

**Evidence status:** Purely experimental. No validated clinical use. The following concepts are intended for research structuring and future pharmacogenetic risk assessment, not for diagnostics.

**Pharmacogenetic screening before FQ prescription (future concept):**
The Reinhardt study [2] identifies AIFM1 and IDH2 as molecular off-targets of fluoroquinolones. Genetic polymorphisms in these proteins may explain why some patients are particularly susceptible to FQ toxicity. Future pharmacogenetic screening before FQ prescription could theoretically identify high-risk patients — analogous to HLA-B*5701 testing before abacavir or CYP2D6 testing before codeine.

Additionally relevant: polymorphisms in clearance enzymes (CYP family, NAT1/NAT2 acetylator status), which could slow hepatic FQ elimination and prolong tissue accumulation [4], [48].

**Antioxidant defense genes — the cellular “fire department”:** In addition to the direct FQ binding partners (AIFM1, IDH2) and the clearance enzymes (CYP), polymorphisms in the antioxidant defense deserve special attention:

| Gene | Function | Relevance to FQAD vulnerability |
|---|---|---|
|**SOD2**(superoxide dismutase 2, mitochondrial) | Converts superoxide (O₂•⁻) to H₂O₂ — the first and most critical step in mitochondrial ROS detoxification | In SOD2 polymorphisms (e.g. Ala16Val, rs4880), mitochondrial superoxide detoxification is slowed down. When FQ trigger the "oxidative fire" (Front 1, D5), SOD2 genetics decide whether the cell can extinguish the fire or slip into the FQAD cascade [34] |
|**GST genes**(Glutathione S-transferase: GSTM1, GSTT1, GSTP1) | Phase II detoxification: conjugate oxidative damage (lipid peroxides, protein carbonyls) to glutathione for excretion | GSTM1/GSTT1 null genotypes (homozygous deletions, prevalence 20–50% depending on ethnicity) represent complete loss of function. In FQ-induced oxidative stress, these patients lack the second line of defense after SOD2 |

These genes do not determine FQ exposure or clearance, but rather the cell's ability to cope with the resulting oxidative damage. A patient with SOD2-Ala16Val + GSTM1 null genotype has a biochemically doubly weakened "fire department" - the oxidative fire (front 1) escalates faster and more severely with the same FQ dose than in a patient with intact defense genes. SOD2 and GST polymorphisms are testable via standard pharmacogenetics panels (no whole-exome sequencing required).

**Personalized binding simulation (in silico pipeline):**
For patients with available genomic data (e.g. from whole-exome sequencing or consumer genotyping), the following bioinformatics pipeline is theoretically feasible:

| step | Method | Input → Output | hurdle |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Variant identification | Search genomic data for SNPs in AIFM1, IDH2, CYP enzymes, NAT1/NAT2 | Raw data → List of relevant variants | Consumer arrays (23andMe and similar) do not read the entire gene; only preselected SNPs. Rare variants are often not recorded. Complete coverage only by whole-exome sequencing [48] |
| 2. DNA→Protein Translation | Determine the amino acid sequence of each variant via UniProt/dbSNP | DNA letter → amino acid exchange in protein | Not every DNA variant changes the protein (silent mutations) |
| 3. Protein folding | ColabFold (AlphaFold2-based): calculate mutated AIFM1 protein as a 3D structure [49] | FASTA sequence → .pdb file (3D model) | ~15–30 min computing time; free in Google Colab |
| 4. Molecular Docking | DiffDock (MIT): Dock ciprofloxacin/levofloxacin into the personalized binding pocket [50] | .pdb + SMILES code → binding affinity (confidence score) | Multiple runs necessary for reproducibility |
| 5. Comparison with wild type | Perform identical pipeline with standard AIFM1 protein (reference sequence) | Control score vs. patient score | Only the difference is meaningful; an isolated score cannot be interpreted |

**Result Interpretation:** If DiffDock shows that ciprofloxacin binds significantly more strongly in the individual AIFM1 variant than in the wild type, this would be a bioinformatic indication of increased personal susceptibility to FQ-induced ETC blockade. This is not evidence of causality, but rather a hypothesis generator for further research.

**Critical limitations:**
- Consumer genotyping (23andMe, MyHeritage) only captures pre-selected SNPs. A negative result (no error found) does not rule out a variant — the chip may never have read the location [48].
- AlphaFold models are predictions, not experimentally solved structures. Binding simulations are approximations, not exact measurements [49], [50].
- The entire pipeline is a research tool, not a diagnostic test. It cannot diagnose FQAD or justify a personal treatment decision.

**Ex vivo challenge (approach 3) as a potential key innovation — detailed protocol:**

*Setup:* Blood is taken from the affected person; Live PBMCs (lymphocytes, monocytes — high mitochondrial density) are isolated.
-**Sample A (Control):** Cells untreated
-**Sample B (provocation):** Cells were treated with ciprofloxacin in a therapeutic concentration (~7.5 µmol/L, corresponding to plasma Cmax).

*What is measured (the “AIFM1/IDH2 fingerprint”):*
-**Mitochondrial membrane potential:** Fluorescent dyes (e.g. JC-1, TMRM) show whether the “battery” of the mitochondria breaks down under ciprofloxacin. In those affected with a vulnerable AIFM1 binding pocket: an above-average drop in voltage
-**Real-time respirometry (Seahorse):** Live measurement of oxygen consumption. In genetically vulnerable patients: Oxygen consumption drops immediately under ciprofloxacin, while healthy cells compensate for the stress
-**IDH2 stress metabolomics:** Testing whether patient cells on ciprofloxacin immediately divert citrate via the rescue pathway (reverse carboxylation) — direct evidence that IDH2 is blocked in the cells of this specific individual [2]

*Why more meaningful than conventional tests:*
- *Individual sensitivity:* A genetic test (approach 5) only says that a variant exists. This functional test shows what the variant does — supporting the hypothesis of a causal connection
- *No patient risk:* The damage only occurs in the Petri dish
- *Objective evidence:* If a patient's cells collapse at a dose that other cells can easily tolerate, this is objective, laboratory evidence of pathological intolerance

**Legal and diagnostic relevance:** A standardized ex vivo challenge protocol could serve as a long-term functional confirmatory FQAD test — objective, reproducible, without risk to the patient. This would be the operationalization of the missing “diagnostic gold standard” that the model declares as non-existent in Section II. In today's statutory health insurance standard, FQAD is purely a diagnosis of exclusion. The aim of the translational research (AIFM1/IDH2 fingerprint, lactate-pyruvate ratio) is to make FQAD an objectively positive diagnosis in the medium term.

**Critical limitation - time window of ex-vivo challenge:** If the individual vulnerability is purely genetic (shape of the AIFM1/IDH2 binding pocket), the test will be effective even years after the damage. However, if the vulnerability at the time of exposure was epigenetic or driven by a temporary cellular deficiency state (e.g. magnesium low during ingestion), the reproduced PBMCs taken today could survive the test in the test tube unscathed - a false-negative finding. This distinction (genetic-permanent vs. situational-temporary) is essential for future research interpretation.

#### CPET warning

Spiroergometry (CPET) is potentially contraindicated in suspected post-exertional malaise (PEM), as maximal exercise can lead to severe worsening of symptoms that lasts for months [23]. Instead, submaximal testing (6-minute walk test, grip strength) or a 2-day CPET exclusively in highly specialized ME/CFS centers.

## 3.1 Damage Stabilization (🟢 Routine / 🟡 Individual)

### Phase A — Damage Stabilization (🟢 Routine / 🟡 Individual)

>**Level of evidence:**🟢 Close to guidelines (avoiding further FQ, correcting defects). 🟡 Individualized (dysautonomia management, crash protocol — transfer from ME/CFS).

| aspect | Description |
|---|---|
|**Target**| Prevent further decompensation |
|**Clinical Status**| Close to guidelines; Transfer from mitochondrial medicine [21] |
|**Major Uncertainty**| No validated data on which measures causally change the course of FQAD |
|**Endpoint**| Symptom stability, avoidance of acute deterioration |
|**Risk**| Progressive decompensation → advanced diagnostics |

Specific measures:

- Strict avoidance of other fluoroquinolones and potentially mitotoxic substances [9], [10]
- Systematic search for aggravating factors: deficiencies, sleep disorder, dysautonomia, malnutrition, endocrine problems, additional neuropathy, renal insufficiency
- Avoidance of catabolic stress: long fasting, dehydration, infection decompensation [21]

#### Prioritized Risk Factor Ranking: What Makes the Damage Worse

A deficiency does not cause the tendon to rupture, but it does deprive the body of the only way to absorb or repair the chemical impact. The following ranking list sorts according to immediate danger:

| Rank | Condition / defect | Category | Pathobiochemical escalation mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|
|**1**|**Intracellular magnesium deficiency**| Mineral (critical) | FQ actively rob Mg²⁺ (chelation, see D2). If there is a lack of free Mg²⁺, ions are ripped directly from enzymes (topoisomerase) and collagen. Mg²⁺ is missing as an antagonist against toxic Ca²⁺ influx into cells [4], [51] |
|**2**|**renal insufficiency + dehydration**| Organ (critical) | Blocked waste disposal: Levofloxacin in particular (almost 100% renal) accumulates massively with weak eGFR → prolonged tissue exposure and ion trapping (see D3) [9], [13] |
|**3**|**Zinc deficiency**| Mineral (high) | Missing MMP brake: TIMPs absolutely require Zn²⁺ as a cofactor. FQ also chelate zinc → enzymatic tissue destruction continues unabated (see D2, TIMPs) [13], [34] |
|**4**|**Simultaneous corticosteroid use**| Medicated (high) | Double structural attack: Corticosteroids weaken collagen formation independently of FQ. In combination, the risk of tendon rupture increases. Officially recognized high-risk factor [9], [10], [13] |
|**5**|**B12 and folic acid deficiency**| Vitamin (high) | Defenseless nerve cell: B12 is essential for myelin sheath and cellular energy. If it is missing, if FQ disrupts the ion channels, the nerve cell dies instead of regenerating → SFN risk increases [5] |
|**6**|**Low muscle mass/underweight**| Metabolism (moderate-high) | Dose trap: Little muscle mass → little creatinine → falsely “perfect” eGFR → no dose reduction → toxic load for remaining tissue massively increased. Use cystatin C instead of creatinine (see D3) |
|**7**|**Vitamin D deficiency**| Vitamin (moderate) | Lack of immune/bone control: Vitamin D modulates inflammation and Ca²⁺ incorporation. Deficiency worsens the baseline for tissue repair and fuels SASP chronicity |
|**8**|**Catabolic Stress / Long Fasting**| Metabolism (moderate) | Self-consumption: Cells in emergency power (glycolysis) constantly need energy. Fasting forces muscle protein burning → ammonia → brain fog, sarcopenia, accelerated tissue breakdown [4], [35] |
|**9**|**Continuous stress + social invalidation**| Neurological (moderate) | Mast cell radical amplifier: Chronic sympathetic nerve excess (also through invalidation) fires up unstable mast cells and artificially keeps the inflammatory loop alive [19], [22] |

The absolute worst defects are those that leave the FQ's direct point of attack (Mg²⁺) defenseless and rob the body of its chemical brakes (Zn²⁺). The vitamin deficiencies (B12, D) are not the cause of the damage in the acute phase, but are the main reason why the body then slips into chronic disease because there is a lack of building materials for reconstruction.

#### Pharmacologic Enablers: Pre-medications that escalate FQAD risk

Medications taken in the months or years before FQ administration can unnoticed rob the body of the armor it needs to absorb the toxic impact. They don't cause FQAD directly, but they precondition the system in a toxic way.

| Pre-medication | Toxic overlap with FQ mechanics | Clinical consequence |
|---|---|---|
|**Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs: pantoprazole, omeprazole)**| PPIs lower stomach acid → intestines have difficulty absorbing Mg²⁺ → officially recognized side effect: hypomagnesemia after long-term use. Additionally: Dysbiosis preparation (Leaky Gut, MCAS soil) | Patient goes into FQ therapy with chronically empty Mg²⁺ stores → chelation effect hits unprotected tissue. One of the most common and dangerous enablers. |
|**Statins (Simvastatin, Atorvastatin)**| Statins block the body's own Q10 production → mitochondria are already energetically damaged. Known myotoxicity (muscle pain to rhabdomyolysis) | FQ-ETC blockade hits muscle cells that are already running at their limit due to Q10 deficiency → immediate energy collapse, deep weakness, increased risk of rhabdomyolysis |
|**Bisphosphonates (Alendronate/Fosamax)**| Have a massive impact on bone metabolism and Ca²⁺ balance; are deposited in the bone for years; known long-term NW: chronic bone/joint/muscle pain | FQ-MMP attack hits a bone-tendon framework (enthesis) that has already been structurally altered and mechanically stiffened by bisphosphonates → potentiated therapy-resistant joint/tendon damage |
|**NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Diclofenac)**| NSAIDs displace FQ from plasma proteins and potentiate FQ-induced GABA receptor blockade in the CNS. Explicit warning in the package insert. | Massively increased risk of neurotoxic attacks, seizures, panic attacks, permanent CNS damage if taken promptly
|**Benzodiazepines (diazepam, lorazepam) / centrally acting psychotropic drugs**| Chronic benzo use breaks down GABA receptors (tolerance). FQ blocks exactly these receptors in an additional toxic way “Cold withdrawal at the cellular level”: The nervous system loses all self-regulation ability → severe neuropsychiatric FQAD courses (extreme restlessness, insomnia, dysautonomia) |

**Clinical consequence:** The medication history (particularly PPI long-term therapy, statins, bisphosphonates, NSAIDs, benzodiazepines) is mandatory before every FQ prescription and for phenotyping if FQAD already exists. A patient who has taken pantoprazole + simvastatin for years enters FQ therapy with depleted magnesium and previously damaged mitochondria - the risk of a severe, chronic course is massively increased.
-**Dysautonomia management:** In some of those affected, dysautonomous patterns and exercise intolerance may be clinically relevant. Non-pharmacological procedures such as pacing, HRV biofeedback or autonomously oriented rehabilitation are plausible but not validated support approaches for FQAD [22]. The evidence comes from small studies of dysautonomia, anxiety disorders and pain; FQAD-specific data is missing.

#### Acute relapse management (crash protocol)

If a patient presents with severe acute stress decompensation (massive increase in pain, cognitive decline, weakness):

- Absolute protection from stimuli (sensory, physical, cognitive)
- No further diagnostic stress tests in this phase
- Short-term H1/H2 blockade in particular with mast cell involvement (cetirizine + famotidine), electrolyte balancing
- No graded exercise, no physiotherapy during acute decompensation
- Monitoring until restabilization, then reevaluation of the load limit

This crash protocol is clinically pragmatic but not FQAD-specific validated; it is based on ME/CFS acute management [23].

#### Stress management: Why “training through pain” is dangerous

In the case of complaints associated with fluoroquinolone, forced exercise to the point of pain is more risky than sensible. In the case of tendon pain, authorities expressly recommend that you protect the affected structure and avoid unnecessary strain because the risk of rupture can be increased [9], [10], [13].

**Differentiated loading rules according to endophenotype:**

| axis | Load rule | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
|**tendons/connective tissue**| No forced strain, no “biting through” when in pain. Protection especially in the acute phase. Eccentric therapy only after ultrasound clearance (see organ axis 4) | Best documented; Product information and authorities explicitly warn [9], [13] |
|**Neuropathy/Dysautonomia**| Overload plausibly harmful; symptom-guided dosage | Plausible, but less hard evidence than with tendons |
|**PEM/stress intolerance**| Pacing is absolute priority; Graded exercise is contraindicated in PEM [23] | Transfer from ME/CFS literature |
|**Neuropsychiatric**| Mental stress can increase pain, fatigue and dysautonomia | Biopsychosocial pain model; no FQAD-specific evidence |
**Rest ≠ complete immobility.**Total inactivity has its own disadvantages (deconditioning, circulatory breakdown, muscle breakdown, pain chronification). The golden mean: symptom-guided activity without provocation, without forced increase, without “it has to hurt”.

#### Warning: Risk of rhabdomyolysis when re-exercising after severe deconditioning

In those affected with severe fluoroquinolone-associated muscle weakness, bed rest or severely limited exercise capacity, even slight re-exertion - short standing, sitting up or a few minutes of activity - can in rare cases lead to severe muscle damage (rhabdomyolysis). This is particularly true in cases of severe deconditioning, dehydration or pre-existing muscle vulnerability.

**Warning signs (emergency - immediate medical evaluation on the same day):**

- Rapidly increasing muscle pain over hours to days
- Unusually severe weakness or sudden loss of function
-**Dark urine**(brown to cola colored — myoglobin in urine)
- Increased temperature/feeling feverish
- Swelling, tenderness or feeling of tension in the muscles
- Significant deterioration after relatively minor stress

**Immediate diagnosis:** CK (creatine kinase - massively increased in rhabdomyolysis), creatinine (kidney function - myoglobin can cause acute kidney failure), electrolytes (potassium - life-threatening hyperkalemia possible), urine status (myoglobinuria).

**Disclaimer:** This is not normal muscle soreness and not the typical FQAD crash/PEM (which results from excess lactate and mitochondrial emergency flow). Rhabdomyolysis is an acute muscle breakdown with potentially life-threatening complications (kidney failure, cardiac arrhythmia) and requires immediate inpatient treatment.

**Practical Reloading Precautions:**

- No forced increase in mobilization after prolonged bed rest
- Exercise only very carefully, symptom-guided and in minimal steps
- For previous episodes with dark urine or extreme aggravation of pain: special restraint
- Ensure adequate fluid intake (unless there are medical reasons against it)
- Arrange a low-threshold CK check if muscle damage is suspected
This warning does not mean that every activity is dangerous. It points out that in severely affected patients, forced or too rapid re-exertion can in individual cases lead to serious, possibly permanently deteriorating muscle damage.

## 3.2 Organ axes — parallel treatment paths (🟡 Individualized)

### Organ axes — parallel treatment paths

A realistic regeneration plan is primarily tissue specific. The following axes require their own, parallel treatment paths.

#### 1) Nephrological axis

Proteinuria monitoring, eGFR progression. Kidney-adapted diet for protein loss. Nephrological co-care for creatinine increases [1].

#### 2) Neurological-autonomic axis

Small fiber neuropathy, peripheral neuropathy, dysautonomia. Symptom-oriented therapy: Gabapentinoids for neuropathic pain - taking into account the tolerability restrictions mentioned at the beginning (see Section I), starting with fractions of the standard dose [5]. Beta-blockers for POTS, fluid and salt management for orthostasis [22]. If GPCR autoantibodies are detected: if necessary, immunoadsorption in a university setting as an experimental option [15], [16].

**Sleep-disordered breathing — a commonly overlooked problem:**

Those affected report nightly drops in SpO₂ (measurable with a pulse oximeter). In organically healthy lungs, the FQAD model explains two mechanisms:

*Diaphragm exhaustion (hypoventilation):* The diaphragm requires large amounts of ATP to breathe for hours at night. When the respiratory chain is blocked and anaerobic emergency flow occurs, the respiratory muscle becomes fatigued - breathing becomes increasingly shallow during sleep (hypoventilation) and blood oxygen measurably drops [35].

*Central sleep apnea (dysautonomia):* The automatic breathing drive during deep sleep is controlled by the autonomic nervous system. In severe dysautonomia, the brain temporarily "forgets" to send the breathing impulse - breathing pauses (central apneas) occur in which the remaining oxygen is used up without replenishment [22].

*Diagnostics:* Pulse oximetry screening at home (over several nights); in the case of documented declines: polysomnography in the sleep laboratory to differentiate between obstructive, central and mixed sleep apnea as well as hypoventilation.
*Therapy:* The solution is primarily mechanical, not chemical: CPAP or BiPAP breathing support relieves the exhausted diaphragm of work and prevents breathing pauses. The sole administration of high-dose oxygen via a nasal cannula without supporting the breathing mechanics does not solve the problem of diaphragm exhaustion and remains an incalculable risk in the case of blocked mitochondria due to the risk of oxidative stress (Fenton reaction) [34].

#### 3) Gastrointestinal-immunological axis

Fluoroquinolones are primarily broad-spectrum antibiotics - the resulting dysbiosis can act as an independent, chronically relevant axis of damage. This axis is hypothetically classified in FQAD (biologically plausible, FQAD-specific has hardly been investigated); the prevalence of clearly defined gut-driven courses in FQAD is not known [18]–[20].

**Mechanism:** Antibiotic-induced dysbiosis → loss of protective bacteria (bifidobacteria, lactobacilli) → barrier disruption (“leaky gut”) → bacterial translocation (toxins in the bloodstream) → systemic inflammation → mast cell activation. Fluoroquinolones can also activate mast cells directly via MRGPRX2 [19]. This could explain why intestinal problems in FQAD are often accompanied by allergy-like reactions, cognitive decline or joint pain.

**Regulatory T cells (Tregs) as microbiome victims — the Treg mast cell collapse:**

In healthy people, mast cells are kept in check by regulatory T cells (Tregs). Tregs act as an immunological “brake” by releasing anti-inflammatory messenger substances (especially IL-10) that signal to the mast cells: stay calm. Fluoroquinolones could attack this brake via two converging pathways:

1.**Microbiome loss → Butyrate deficiency:** The broad-spectrum antibiotic destroys commensal intestinal bacteria that produce short-chain fatty acids (especially butyrate). Butyrate is essential for the differentiation and survival of Tregs. Without butyrate, the Treg population could collapse [18], [46].
2.**Oxidative stress → direct T cell damage:** The FQ-induced mitochondrial energy deficiency and the mass ROS production (see D5, front 1) also directly damage the sensitive T lymphocytes [4], [34].
**Consequence:** If the Treg brake breaks down, mast cells degranulate uncontrollably at the slightest stimulus. The nervous system is flooded with histamine and cytokines, the stimulus threshold drops - the development of MCAS and MCS is immunologically disinhibited.

**Causal chain:** FQ toxicity → butyrate deficiency + ROS → Treg collapse → mast cell disinhibition (MCAS) → MCS.

**Diagnostics of Treg deficiency (🟡 Special diagnostics, immunology):**

| Parameters | Method | What he shows | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
|**CD4⁺/CD25⁺/FoxP3⁺ (Tregs)**| Flow cytometry (FACS) of lymphocyte subpopulations | Quantification of functional regulatory T cells. FoxP3 is the transcription factor that identifies true, suppressive Tregs | Significantly reduced fraction → immunological disinhibition proven in laboratory chemistry → supports Treg mast cell collapse as the cause of MCAS |
|**Interleukin-10 (IL-10)**| Serum/plasma | Primary anti-inflammatory messenger Tregs | With Treg deficiency often reduced → lack of immunological brake objectified |
|**Tryptase / Methylhistamine**| Serum (tryptase) / 24h urine (methylhistamine) | Proof that the mast cells actually degranulate in an uncontrolled manner | In combination with reduced Tregs: confirmation of the Treg-mast cell axis |

The CD4⁺/CD25⁺/FoxP3⁺ determination is available from specialist immunological laboratories (not routine blood work). The combination of decreased Tregs + increased tryptase/methylhistamine + clinical MCAS symptoms results in a complete biological causal chain that goes beyond a pure description of symptoms.

**Molecular Mechanics of Mast Cell Activation — DAMPs and Calcium Influx:**

Mast cell activation in FQAD likely occurs through at least three converging pathways:
1.**DAMPs (Damage-Associated Molecular Patterns):** Damaged mitochondria release molecular "alarm waste" (mtDNA fragments, cardiolipin, ATP) - so-called DAMPs, which act as endogenous danger signals. These DAMPs activate pattern recognition receptors (TLR9, NLRP3) on mast cells and signal: “Tissue dies here” → inflammatory cascade [4], [34].
2.**Direct MRGPRX2 pathway:** Ciprofloxacin directly activates the MRGPRX2 receptor on mast cells, triggering immediate, non-IgE-mediated degranulation (histamine release) — without classic allergy sensitization [19].
3.**Intracellular calcium influx:** Ciprofloxacin induces a rapid intracellular Ca²⁺ increase in mast cells, which acts as a direct physical switch for exocytosis (granule release). In combination with the FQ-induced NMDA overactivation and GABA blockade (see section C, axis 4), a systemic Ca²⁺ excess is created, which permanently lowers the mast cell degranulation threshold.

**Important distinction:** Proven dysbiosis is not evidence of mtDNA damage or collagen breakdown — it is a separate, parallel axis of damage. A commercial stool microbiome test shows who lives in the gut, but not what damage the imbalance has done to the barrier.

|**Zonulin (blood/stool)**| Clinically relevant barrier dysfunction (leaky gut) | IGeL / special laboratory |

**Probiotics for FQAD — The Histamine Trap:**

Many commercially available probiotics (particularly favorable Lactobacillus strains) produce their own histamine in the intestine. If mast cell activation is present, the wrong probiotic can trigger severe inflammatory attacks, cognitive deterioration or pain [18], [20]. Low-histamine bacterial strains (e.g. Bifidobacterium longum, Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG - selection based on individual tolerance) are the safer option if MCAS is suspected. The document does not name any specific preparations as recommendations.
**Postbiotics as an alternative to probiotics:** Instead of swallowing live bacteria and hoping for their survival, the curative horizon (Section XIV) refers to synthetic postbiotics: the pure metabolites of healthy bacteria (short-chain fatty acids such as butyrate) that directly stabilize the intestinal barrier and act as anti-inflammatory signaling molecules [46]. This is research, not a therapeutic available today.

>**Level of evidence:**🟡 Butyrate production by prebiotics is microbiologically established. The specific use in FQAD/MCAS is not validated. Basic rule: “Low and Slow”, introduce one at a time, stop if it worsens.

The histamine trap extends not only to probiotic capsules, but also to foods: fermented foods (yoghurt, kefir, sauerkraut, kombucha) are created through fermentation, during which a lot of histamine is naturally produced - often a direct trigger for flare-ups in the case of leaky gut and unstable mast cells.

**Therapy if clinically suspected:** Mast cell stabilizers (cromoglicin acid, ketotifen; caveat: GI irritation as a side effect), low-histamine diet (avoidance of aged cheese, wine, tomatoes, fermented foods), targeted microbiome build-up only with low-histamine strains and after diagnostics [18]–[20].

**Severe mast cell activation — when even water cannot be tolerated:**

In extreme cases, those affected report reactions to almost all foods and even still water. This is often clinically dismissed as psychosomatic, but it has an organic basis: in severe MCAS, mast cells no longer only fire in response to chemical allergens, but also in response to physical stimuli - mechanical gastric distension (even from a sip of water), temperature differences in the drink or osmotic differences are sufficient to cause unstable mast cells to degranulate [19]. The root causes of chronicity: senescent cells in the intestinal tissue constantly release SASP inflammatory substances, which keep mast cells in a state of alert 24 hours a day; At the same time, a barrier damaged by dysbiosis (leaky gut) keeps the immune system in constant stress.
Mast cell stabilizers (H1/H2 blockers, cromoglicic acid, ketotifen) do not repair mitochondrial damage or the intestinal barrier, but rather create a "chemical armor" around the fragile mast cells so that they do not degranulate immediately with minimal stimuli. For many sufferers, this symptom control is the only way to maintain food or fluids at all - an essential measure of damage stabilization (Phase A), which may remain necessary in the long term or permanently as long as the underlying cell damage is not repaired.

**Multiple Chemical Sensitivity (MCS) as a result of the MCAS/GABA double blockade:**

>**Level of evidence:**🟡 Plausible. Mechanistically justifiable from the combination of MCAS literature and GABA neurotoxicity data; FQAD-specific not systematically examined.

MCS (hypersensitivity to chemicals, odors, fumes in concentrations that healthy people can easily tolerate) is frequently reported by those affected by FQAD, but has not yet been discussed as an independent complication in the document. The pathogenesis could result from the convergence of two damage mechanisms:

1.**Immunological component (MCAS):** Chronically unstable mast cells with a lowered degranulation threshold (due to DAMPs, Treg loss and MRGPRX2 sensitization) respond to minimal chemical stimuli with histamine release.
2.**Neurological component (GABA filter failure):** FQ competitively block GABA-A receptors in the CNS [5]. The resulting neuronal hyperexcitability - especially in the limbic system (amygdala, olfactory cortex) - could lead to the central nervous system no longer adequately filtering chemical environmental stimuli and misinterpreting them as life-threatening danger signals.

The combination of immunological overreaction (MCAS) and neuronal misinterpretation (GABA deficit) could explain why MCS occurs so frequently and so severely in those affected by FQAD - and why purely immunological therapies (mast cell stabilizers) alone are often not enough: the neurological filter disorder persists independently of mast cell stabilization. Therapeutically, a dual approach (MCAS stabilization + neural desensitization/GABA support) should be considered, although there is no FQAD-specific evidence for any of these approaches.

**MCAS Stabilization Protocol (10-step approach):**

There is no single solution to toxically induced MCAS — it requires a multi-pronged approach: shield with medication, reduce histamine supply, calm an overstimulated nervous system. Each change must be tested individually. Evidence status: transferred from general MCAS literature; FQAD-specific not validated [18]–[20].

*Medicinal basis (medical support):*
1.**H1 + H2 blocker combination:** Cetirizine/desloratadine (H1, skin/respiratory) plus famotidine (H2, gastrointestinal). Only the combination adequately protects both types of receptors.
2.**Oral mast cell stabilizers:** Cromoglicic acid or ketotifen coats the mast cell and prevents degranulation. Slow dosing is mandatory - even these medications can initially cause irritation.
3.**Leukotriene antagonists:** Montelukast cuts off the second axis of inflammation (leukotrienes) when H1/H2 blockers alone are not enough.

*Nutrition and intestinal axis:*
4.**Consistent low-histamine elimination diet:** Avoiding fermented, matured and warmed foods (cheese, red wine, tomatoes, soy sauce). Doesn't cure mast cells, but takes the constant pressure out of the system.
5.**DAO enzyme substitution before meals:** Diamine oxidase (DAO) breaks down histamine in the intestines. In antibiotic-induced dysbiosis, the body's own DAO production is often reduced. DAO capsules (e.g. Daosin) shortly before eating act as enzymatic histamine disposal.
6.**Gentle barrier stabilization:** Postbiotics (butyrate) or L-glutamine (test extremely carefully and in low doses in FQAD) can stabilize tight junctions [46]. No irritating standard probiotics (see histamine trap above).

*Nervous system and environment:*
7.**Trigger identification via symptom diary:** Hidden triggers: strong smells (perfume, cleaning products), temperature fluctuations, additives in medications (titanium dioxide, dyes).
8.**Soothe autonomic axis:** Vagus nerve activation (4-7-8 breathing, gentle humming, non-invasive vagal stimulation if necessary). No extreme cold/stress training. Sympathetic excess directly fuels mast cells [22].
9.**Strictly avoid PEM:** Metabolic crashes (lactate buildup, cellular stress) are one of the strongest internal mast cell triggers. Pacing is also active MCAS therapy [23].

*Gentle cofactors:*
10.**Buffered Vitamin C:** Most important natural cofactor for DAO production and histamine breakdown. Regular ascorbic acid can irritate the stomach and mast cells — hence calcium ascorbate or liposomal vitamin C in small doses. No high-dose infusions.

**Calcium and magnesium — a common misconception:**

A common misconception is the assumption that if you have FQAD you have to reduce calcium in your diet because intracellular calcium influx triggers cell death (apoptosis). The pathobiochemical reality is more nuanced:

*The problem is intracellular, not dietary:* Cell death occurs due to calcium buildup inside the cell and in the mitochondria. The calcium from the diet ends up in the blood, the level of which the body keeps exactly constant through homeostasis - if necessary by breaking down bones. Eating less calcium does not lower intracellular calcium but increases the risk of osteoporosis — particularly dangerous in immobile FQAD patients.
*Why cells “drown” in calcium (the ATP problem):* Healthy cells constantly pump excess calcium via calcium ATPases — these pumps require ATP. When the respiratory chain is blocked and there is anaerobic emergency power, ATP is missing, the pumps stop, and calcium floods the cell uncontrollably [4], [35].

*The true antagonist - Magnesium:* Magnesium and calcium are antagonists on the ion channels (especially NMDA receptors). Adequate magnesium levels block uncontrolled calcium influx, even in the case of ATP deficiency. Since fluoroquinolones bind magnesium through chelation [4], many of those affected suffer from intracellular magnesium deficiency, which further escalates the calcium problem.

*Consequence:* Do not reduce calcium in the diet. Check magnesium status instead — serum magnesium alone is not enough (only shows extracellular Mg); a whole blood mineral analysis (intracellular magnesium) is more informative. Magnesium is already part of the basic diagnosis of statutory health insurance (level 1); the whole blood analysis is IGeL/special laboratory.

#### 4) Musculoskeletal-tendinous axis

**Pathomechanism:** Fluoroquinolones upregulate matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs), which actively degrade tendon collagen [13]. At the same time, damaged fibroblasts go into senescence and stop producing collagen. The result: enzymatic decay with a simultaneous stop in production - a gap that cannot be closed by adding building materials alone.

**The retrograde signaling chain: Why damaged mitochondria rewrite the collagen blueprint in the cell nucleus (🟡 Plausible)**

The clinically observed phenomenon - after FQ damage, tendon tissue no longer grows parallel and elastic, but rather chaotic, thickened and scarred (chronic tendinopathy/fibrosis) - is caused by a chain reaction between mitochondrial and nuclear DNA:

| step | Location | What happens |
|---|---|---|
|**1. Origin: mtDNA damage**| Mitochondria | FQ inhibit mitochondrial topoisomerase II → mtDNA damage [2], [3]. The mtDNA does not contain any collagen blueprints, only the blueprints of the respiratory chain complexes. Consequence: Cell hardly produces ATP, but massive ROS (oxidative stress) |
|**2. Retrograde signaling**| Cytoplasm → cell nucleus | The oxidative stress from the broken mitochondria floods the entire cell and penetrates into the nucleus. In addition, released DAMPs (mtDNA fragments, cardiolipin) signal to the cell nucleus: “Energy crisis, tissue damage, activate emergency program” |
|**3. Epigenetic shift in nDNA**| Cell nucleus (nuclear DNA) | ROS + energy deficiency alters the epigenetic switches (DNA methylation, histone acetylation) on the collagen genes in nuclear DNA. Epigenetics determines which genes can be read - the oxidative stress “pastes over” the correct blueprints
|**4. Collagen Type Switch**| Tenocytes | The epigenetically misprogrammed tenocytes switch off genes for healthy, parallel**type I collagen**(elastic, load-transmitting) and instead activate**type III collagen**(thin, rigid, unstructured - "emergency collagen" for rapid wound healing) + MMP overproduction [13] |
|**5. Chronic tendinopathy**| tendon tissue | Result: Instead of elastic, parallel fibers, chaotic, knotted scar tissue (fibrosis) is created. The tendon is thickened but mechanically weaker — it can no longer transmit tensile forces along one axis, which increases the risk of rupture |

**Clinical consequence:** The collagen blueprint defect is located in the nuclear DNA (nDNA), but is almost always the direct result of oxidative fire from the damaged mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA). This explains why isolated collagen supplementation (gelatin, hydrolyzate) does not solve the problem: the building material is supplied, but the blueprint in the cell nucleus is incorrectly programmed. The therapy must therefore primarily reduce oxidative stress (Mg²⁺, antioxidants) and restore mitochondrial function (PGC-1α activation) so that the epigenetic switch in the cell nucleus can switch back to type I collagen.
**The MMP problem — why diet alone is not enough:** There are currently no approved agents or dietary supplements that specifically and safely stop MMPs in FQAD. Herbal “MMP inhibitors” (curcumin, OPC, quercetin, green tea extract) from forums have not been proven for this level of damage and are not listed as therapy in the model. The Curative Horizon (Section XIV) outlines siRNA hydrogels as a future concept: locally injected gels that genetically silence MMP blueprints in tissue [45]. Indirectly, reducing redox stress (diet, avoiding blood sugar peaks) can reduce the long-term inflammatory stress on the cells and thus weaken MMP upregulation - an indirect, not a direct lever [34].

**Collagen hydrolyzate - realistic assessment:** Useful as an easily digestible protein source (glycine, proline) to support and prevent catabolic tissue breakdown. However, it will not stop or reverse the enzymatic tendon breakdown alone: ​​The problem is not primarily a lack of building blocks, but rather a stop in cell production (senescence) with simultaneous active degradation (MMPs). Visually: truckloads of new bricks don't help when the bricklayers go on strike and an excavator tears down the wall. The absorbed protein is metabolized without being specifically incorporated into the damaged tendon - unless the fibroblasts are given a mechanical remodeling command at the same time.

**Eccentric stress therapy — the central tool for today:**

Normal rest allows pain to subside, but does not build new tissue. Normal strength training can overload the damaged tendon. Eccentric loading is the mechanical middle ground:

*Principle:* The tendon is lengthened slowly and in a controlled manner under tension (example: from standing on your toes, lower your heels extremely slowly). The uniform mechanical pull signals the remaining healthy fibroblasts precisely in which direction they need to weave new collagen fibers. The unstructured scar tissue is realigned — a structural remodeling process that takes months.

*Three hard FQAD conditions:*

| Condition | Requirement | Consequence of non-fulfillment |
|---|---|---|
|**1. Acute phase must be over**| No active inflammation, no acute pain attack | In case of acute irritation: strict protection, no training |
|**2. Ultrasound monitoring before start**| High-resolution ultrasound with elastography: assess collagen thickness, microcracks, inhomogeneity [13] | If collagen is dangerously thinned or microtears: risk of rupture → training contraindicated |
|**3. No PEM**| Documented stress tolerance without post-exertional malaise | If you have a tendency to PEM: Pacing has absolute priority; Eccentric training only after PEM stabilization |

*Protocol (example Achilles tendon):* Start with minimal body weight, extremely slow eccentrics (5-10 seconds per repetition), only 1-2 sets of 5-10 repetitions. Increase only if tolerability is documented for at least 48 hours (no flare-ups, no PEM, no swelling). Physiotherapeutic support recommended. Ultrasound check after 4-6 weeks.

*Differentiation between training pain and FQAD crash:* A slight pulling sensation in the tendon during or shortly after exercise that subsides within hours is to be expected. However, if the exercise results in days of full-body fatigue, cognitive decline, searing nerve pain, or radiating swelling, the metabolic or structural limit has been exceeded → immediate stop, crash protocol (Phase A).

PRP (Platelet-Rich Plasma) is case-dependent and has not been validated for FQAD [13].

**Accelerated Osteoarthritis in Young FQAD Sufferers — Levels of Evidence:**

It is often reported in the community that those affected develop severe osteoarthritis in their 30s or 40s. However, the evidence is tiered:

| Level of evidence | statement |
|---|---|
|**Occupied**| Fluoroquinolones can cause long-lasting damage to tendons, muscles, joints and nerves. Joint pain and swelling are recognized as possible long-lasting side effects [9], [10], [13] |
|**Plausible**| Accelerated development of osteoarthritis can occur secondarily when joint mechanics deteriorate due to tendon, muscle and possibly cartilage/matrix damage and chronic incorrect loading occurs. Older toxicological works describe FQ-induced chondrocyte apoptosis, reduced matrix formation and altered MMP expression in cartilage cells [13] |
|**Not documented**| That fluoroquinolones generally primarily trigger rapidly progressive osteoarthritis as an independent direct effect in humans. The evidence for this is significantly weaker than for tendinopathy |

*Three converging mechanisms (hypothetical):*
1.**Chondrocyte crash:** FQ attack the mitochondria of the cartilage cells → apoptosis or senescence → repair stops in the cartilage. MMP-3 and MMP-13 specifically break down collagen type II (main component of cartilage) from the inside [13].

2.**Mechanical instability:** Softened ligaments and degenerated tendons no longer keep the joint on track → micro-instability → asymmetrical abrasion of the remaining cartilage (comparable to a crooked car tire).

3.**Chronic joint inflammation:** SASP factors from senescent cells maintain smoldering inflammation in the joint, which further accelerates degradation.

The cleanest formulation: "The antibiotic could have damaged tendons, muscles, matrix and possibly cartilage cells; this can lead to altered joint mechanics and thus accelerated wear."

**Cartilage preservation during bed rest - passive micro-mobilization:**

Cartilage is avascular; it is nourished exclusively by diffusion from the synovial fluid. This diffusion process requires rhythmic compression and release — a "pump" through movement. When you are forced to stay in bed, this pump stops working and the cartilage also degenerates due to a lack of nutrients.

*Passive micro-movements (also possible with severe PEM):*
- Slow, passive bending/stretching of the joints (knees, ankles, fingers) while lying down - no use of muscle strength, but gravity or hand guidance
- Heel rocking: Allow the heel to slide minimally forward/back on the mattress (a few centimeters)
- Toe circling: Allow the ankle joint to rotate passively, without muscle tension
- Goal: 2-3 times a day for 2-5 minutes per joint, pain-free, without any effort
- Discontinue if there is pain, swelling or subsequent deterioration

This puts no strain on tendons or the cardiovascular system and does not provoke PEM, but maintains minimal synovial fluid circulation. Physiotherapeutic guidance is recommended.

#### 5) Endocrine axis

With long-term stress, secondary HPA axis dysfunction can become clinically relevant [17]. Evaluate cortisol, DHEA-S and sex hormones in a phenotype-guided manner. Endocrine balancing should, if necessary, be checked before intensive re-conditioning.

#### 6) Neuropsychiatric axis
Anxiety, depression, cognitive dysfunction, sleep disorder. Psychological support, if necessary cognitive behavioral therapy. SSRI/SNRI only with extreme caution (see Section I). The integration of neuropsychiatric care serves to treat real symptoms and coping stress, not to question organic damage [5], [14].

**Social invalidation as a chronification amplifier:**

Devaluation, pressure and disbelief from relatives, friends or doctors (“don’t act like that”, “that can hurt”, “you’re imagining it”) are extremely common among those affected by FQAD. This is not medically harmless: in chronic pain and multisystem diseases, it is well documented that social support is more protective, while invalidation is associated with worse symptom burden, more depression and poorer outcome.

This does not mean that the illness is "just psychological" - on the contrary: authorities recognize that fluoroquinolones can cause long-lasting, disabling and sometimes irreversible damage to nerves, tendons, muscles and the CNS [9], [10]. If someone is constantly invalidated in this situation, social pressure meets real damage. The resulting vicious circle of sleep disturbance, tension, incorrect stress behavior and more pain can measurably increase chronicity.

**Short information for relatives:**

- Fluoroquinolones can rarely but cause serious long-term damage to multiple organ systems. This is recognized by FDA, EMA and BfArM [9], [10].
- “Try harder, it may hurt” is medically counterproductive if there is possible tendon/nerve involvement. If you have tendon problems, rest is explicitly recommended [13].
- "You're imagining it" is a form of invalidation that can worsen the outcome.
- More helpful: “I believe you,” “We’ll see what you can tolerate today,” “We’ll take a symptom-guided approach.”
- The goal is not forced normality, but gradual, measurable stabilization and functional gain.

**Nutritional strategy based on the pathobiochemical model (plausible, not FQAD validated):**

The diet is aimed at three axes: relieving the load on mitochondria, reducing anaerobic emergency power (excess lactate) and stopping catabolic tissue breakdown. Any changeover should be done carefully (“low and slow”) [47].

*1. Mitochondria support and anti-catabolism:*
High-quality protein (eggs, fish, legumes) in sufficient quantities to counteract the breakdown of muscle protein caused by the lack of energy. Omega-3 fatty acids (linseed oil, walnuts, algae oil) support cell membranes and counteract oxidative stress. B vitamins (whole grains if tolerated, green leafy vegetables) as cofactors for energy metabolism. Foods rich in vitamin C (peppers, citrus fruits) for collagen synthesis in damaged tendons and fascia [13], [47]. Collagen hydrolyzate (glycine, proline) can be useful as an easily digestible supplementary protein source, but will not alone stop enzymatic tendon breakdown (see organ axis 4: MSK).

*2. Lactate management (reduction of acid load):*
Avoiding isolated sugars and white flour — these fuel glycolysis (the emergency mode), which leads to rapid lactate increases and can worsen post-exertional malaise [35]. Instead: complex carbohydrates (oats, quinoa, potatoes) that provide stable energy without acute lactate peaks. Green vegetables (spinach, broccoli) provide magnesium and potassium as acid buffers. Berries (blueberries, raspberries) contain antioxidants that can counteract the oxidative cascade (Fenton reaction) [34], [47].

*3. Organ axis-specific nutrition:*
Kidney-adapted when eGFR is compromised: moderate, high-quality protein intake, sparing use of salt. Low-histamine diet if there are signs of mast cell activation (avoidance of aged cheese, wine, tomatoes, fermented foods) [18]–[20]. Digestive relief: light evening meals save energy for nightly repair processes.

*4. Protein cycling (only under medical supervision):*
Alternation of building phases (sufficient protein for muscle maintenance, if necessary with BCAA supplementation) and relief phases (moderately reduced protein intake to relieve mTOR and enable autophagy) [25]. Contraindicated in cases of underweight, severe sarcopenia or unstable adrenal status. Any form of protein or calorie reduction must ensure the preservation of muscle mass; Unintentional sarcopenia would further impair the ability to regenerate [17], [25].

**Disclaimer:** These nutritional principles are biologically plausible but have not been validated in FQAD studies. They do not replace individual nutritional advice.

**Weight loss in FQAD: warning signal, not treatment goal (🟢 clinically relevant)**

The absolute priority is to maintain muscle mass and avoid catabolic stress.

### Quantifying the damage: Why a percentage is not possible

A common patient question is: "What percentage of my cells are damaged?" The model could explain why this is currently unanswerable:

-**No validated overall biomarker**exists that reliably measures the proportion of damaged cells in the body [6].
-**Mosaic Pattern:** Fluoroquinolones do not damage every cell equally. Depending on the endophenotype, tendon cells, small nerve fibers, podocytes or muscle cells are primarily affected - to varying degrees [1], [6].

Instead of absolute cell numbers, the model requires the measurement of the**loss of function**per organ axis:

| Method | What it shows | Practical significance |
|---|---|---|
|**PEM threshold (everyday observation)**| From what duration/intensity of stress does deterioration follow? Best everyday indicator of the extent of mitochondrial blockage; the lower the threshold, the more pronounced the functional failure |
|**Skin punch biopsy (IENF density)**| Actual thinning of small nerve fibers | Quantitative structural evidence of the neurological damage axis |
|**Lactate-pyruvate quotient (exposure)**| Collapse of hepatic lactate clearance under load | Functional evidence of the metabolic emergency power shift |
|**Ultrasonic elastography (tendon)**| Inhomogeneity, cracks, thickening of collagen | Structural evidence of tendinous degeneration |
|**6-minute walk test / grip strength**| Everyday stress reserve | Comparative value for progress observation and regeneration monitoring |

Looking for an absolute percentage is not helpful. Therapy and stabilization are based on the clinical behavior and the measurable endpoints of the affected organs (see Recovery Framework, Section XII).

### Basic principle

The fundamental difference between forum trends and the clinical-translational model: Forums often attempt to "force" healing (through high-dose infusions, aggressive fasting or oxygen pressure). The model positions itself in opposite directions: Goal number 1 is to stabilize damage and prevent further decompensation, not aggressive stimulation of diseased cells.

**Before any experimental intervention, the following applies:** Objective diagnostics and phenotyping (Phase B) → laboratory-guided correction of defects → stabilization → only then, under medical supervision, consider cautious translationally plausible approaches.

### Commercial “Regenerative Medicine” Offerings for FQAD — Evaluation Framework

Desperate patients are increasingly encountering commercial clinics offering specific FQAD treatment programs — typically combinations of IV vitamin infusions, diet/supplement protocols, and "stem cell therapy" or "cellular therapy." Such programs are advertised with success stories, often cost five to six-figure amounts and often require serial or multi-week treatments.

**Core Medical Review:** Marketing and testimonials are not the same as robust evidence. The fact that a practice takes FQAD seriously and offers a plausible recovery narrative is not evidence of effectiveness, safety, or suitability for specific subgroups. The FDA specifically warns that many regenerative/stem cell-based offerings are not approved and have not been proven safe or effective for numerous conditions [9]. The ISSCR (International Society for Stem Cell Research) emphasizes that with commercial stem cell therapies it is often not even clear which cells are given in what quality, dose and processing.
**Typical problem structure:**
- Several different elements are offered together (IV nutrients, diet, supplements, cell therapy) → in the end it is unclear which part will work if improvement occurs
- Strong success narratives, but no transparent published data with inclusion/exclusion criteria, endpoints, side effects and follow-up
- No registered study protocol, no peer-reviewed clinical outcome publication for FQAD
- Treatment is advertised for an area in which there is real medical damage, but no established causal therapy exists - this is precisely where patients are particularly vulnerable

**Benefit-Risk Assessment:**

| Dimension | Review |
|---|---|
| Potential Benefit | Theoretically present (cell/secretome/supportive approaches not biologically absurd), but not reliably proven for FQAD |
| Medical risk | Real and difficult to calculate due to lack of transparency; for stem cell products: infections, immune reactions, tumor risk cannot be ruled out |
| Financial risk | High; Serial treatments with uncertain effect |
| Overall verdict | If there is no study protocol, no publication and no safety documentation: benefit-risk ratio unclear to unfavorable |

**The fair counterposition:** It is possible that individual patients benefit from such programs. But without clean data you don't know who, why, how and at what price. Individual success stories are not enough to classify a therapy as sensibly calculable.

**Checklist: 7 questions before making a decision**

For security reasons, anyone seriously considering such an offer should ask at least the following questions — and insist on clear, verifiable answers:

1.**What cell source is used?**Autologous/allogeneic? MSCs, secretome or something else? Which quality standards apply?
2.**Is there a standard written protocol?**With defined doses, timing and follow-up?
3.**Which side effects/deteriorations are systematically documented?**
4.**Is there published or properly anonymized outcome data specifically for FQAD?**(Not just testimonials, but data with endpoints and follow-up)
5.**Which patients are rejected**because the risk is too high? (If no one gets rejected, that's a warning sign)
6.**What is the plan if things get worse?**Is there an emergency/abort protocol?
7.**Which parts of the program are evidence-based and which are purely experimental?**

If there are no clear, verifiable answers to these questions, that is a strong warning sign.

-**Self-care:** Gastroparesis → special food preparation (flash cooking, Wolfen); PEM → Help with personal care
-**Illness management:** Mg foot baths, UVB therapy, doctor's visits, special meals = high time expenditure (often underestimated!)
-**Everyday organization:** Brain Fog → Organization not possible

*Assessment tips:* Care diary (2 weeks, document every minute of help). Describe the worst days, not the best. Have all findings reports ready.

*Benefits from care level 1:* Relief allowance (131 €/month → household help/shopping), care aids (42 €/month), living space adjustments (up to 4,000 € subsidy, e.g. grab bar for tendon instability). From level 2: care allowance (from €347). Without level of care: Household help is possible as a health insurance benefit (§ 38 SGB V) if the family doctor certifies that housekeeping is not possible due to illness.

## 3.10 Health policy structural reforms: International role models for Germany

> The authorities' previous strategy (sending red hand letters and hoping that doctors read them) has obviously failed - the number of prescriptions fell by ~34% [57], but ~17 million DDD/year (2019) and an estimated 8-9 million DDD (2023) are not acceptable given the documented burden of damage. The following international role models do not rely on hope, but rather on hard system limits.

**1. No-fault damage fund based on the ONIAM model (France) — the most important point for those affected**
In France, the state authority ONIAM (Office National d'Indemnisation des Accidents Médicaux) compensates patients with serious medication injuries regardless of fault (no-fault). The patient does not have to prove medical negligence or manufacturer intent - it is sufficient to prove that the damage was most likely caused by the medication.

*FQAD relevance for Germany:* The year-long, ruinous legal battle against pharmaceutical lawyers (which systematically overburdens those affected by FQAD with PEM, brain fog and disability) is no longer necessary. Fast financial help for care, household and living expenses. The estimated 150,000-450,000 permanent losses justify a dedicated fund. Financing model: Manufacturer levy per FQ pack sold (analogous to the Contergan Foundation).

**2. Delayed prescription / “Wait and See” prescription (UK, NICE guidelines)**

The doctor writes the AB prescription but gives the instruction: "Don't fill it for 3-5 days, only if it gets worse." 70–80% of uncomplicated infections heal spontaneously.

*FQAD relevance:* Would immediately eliminate millions of unnecessary FQ prescriptions for minor infections (uUTI, sinusitis, bronchitis - together ~63% of all FQ prescriptions [63]). The patient has psychological safety (prescription in his pocket), but the pharmacy never sees him in the majority of cases. Not yet established as a guideline standard in Germany.

**3. Real-time AI-powered prescription review (Netherlands)**

When the doctor enters ciprofloxacin into the practice system, software checks the electronic patient file for absolute contraindications in milliseconds.

*FQAD relevance:* Immediate alarm for the most common high-risk constellations that are overlooked in the stress of practice: "Patient is taking corticosteroids at the same time → officially recognized high-risk factor for tendon rupture" (see vulnerability level 7). “Patient takes PPI (Pantoprazole) → Mg²⁺ depletion → FQ toxicity increased” (see pharmacological preload). “Kidney function eGFR <60 → risk of accumulation, especially with levofloxacin” (see cystatin C case). “Age >65 + diabetes → double the risk of seniors”. The prescription is only activated when the doctor actively and justifiably overwrites the warning. The technical infrastructure (ePA, telematics infrastructure) has existed in Germany since 2025 - the algorithms just need to be implemented.

**4. Legal protection for non-regulation (Sweden)**
Many German doctors prescribe FQ for defensive medicine: "What if the cystitis turns into pyelonephritis and the patient sues me because I didn't give an antibiotic?"

*FQAD relevance:* Sweden legally protects doctors if they strictly adhere to guidelines and do not prescribe AB for minor infections. This explicit liability protection is missing in Germany. A legal clarification that non-prescription in accordance with guidelines is not treatment error would immediately reduce the systemic prescribing pressure for minor infections.

**5. Visual risk classes on the packaging + mandatory second opinion**

*Risk classes:* A red warning symbol on the ciprofloxacin package (Class 3: "Reserve antibiotic / Severe, potentially irreversible side effects possible") would motivate patients to ask: "Is there nothing more harmless?" FQ packs currently look identical to harmless antibiotics.

*Mandatory second opinion:* For FQ prescriptions outside of inpatient emergency indications (sepsis, febrile neutropenia), digital approval by an infectiologist or ABS representative (Antibiotic Stewardship) would be conceivable - analogous to the second opinion for spinal surgeries (§ 27b SGB V). The organizational effort (hurdle) would immediately reduce trivial prescriptions to zero.

**6. National FQAD registry (model: Denmark)**

Denmark collects health data centrally and can systematically track the long-term effects of medications using register queries.

*FQAD relevance:* A German FQAD registry (connected to the ePA and the BfArM) would for the first time answer the questions that have been unanswered for 30 years: How many patients who received ciprofloxacin in 2018 had tendon tears, neuropathies or needed a level of care in 2020? What is the actual chronification rate? Which subgroups (CYP1A2, SOD2, Mg status) are particularly at risk? Without a register, all prevalence estimates (see 150,000–450,000) remain model calculations. A register would make the late damages undeniably visible - and thus provide the basis for the no-fault damage fund (point 1).

**Summary: 6-point reform plan for Germany**

| No. | Measure | role model | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | No-Fault Damage Fund | ONIAM (France) | Fast compensation without litigation |
| 2 | Delayed prescription | NICE (UK) | ~70–80% of minor prescriptions eliminated |
| 3 | AI regulation check | e-medication (Netherlands) | Real-time warning of contraindications |
| 4 | Liability protection for non-regulation | Sweden | Defensive medicine pressure eliminated |
| 5 | Risk classes + mandatory second opinion | Analogy § 27b SGB V | Minor prescriptions → zero |
| 6 | National FQAD Registry | Denmark | Late damage visible → evidence base for all other points |

## Practical take-home messages

1. FQAD remains a diagnosis of exclusion - basic diagnostics are mandatory.
2. Goal 1: Damage stabilization and pacing, not "force healing".
3. Think of organ axes separately and treat them in parallel.
4. No off-label senolytics, mitophagy substances or mito-agents outside of studies.
5. Re-conditioning only “low and slow”; with PEM no graded exercise.
6. Titrate CNS medications very carefully and individually.
7. Know and use the crash protocol in the event of acute decompensation.
8. Standard blood counts do not rule out severe mitochondrial dysfunction - the lactate-pyruvate ratio can be helpful in standardized special diagnostics, but is not a validated FQAD biomarker.
9. Any hypothesis about mitophagy, senescence, or reconditioning should be considered subgroup-specific, not “FQAD as a whole.”
10. Diet: minimize sugar/white flour (lactate peaks), sufficient protein against catabolism, kidney-adapted for proteinuria, low histamine if MCAS is suspected - no radical diets without medical supervision. Probiotics only low in histamine and after GI diagnostics (Caution: histamine trap with standard probiotics).
11. Extreme temperature stimuli (sauna, ice bathing) can trigger severe crashes in cases of dysautonomia, SFN and mitochondrial blockade - "low and slow" also applies to environmental stressors.
12. Nocturnal SpO₂ drops in FQAD: pulse oximetry screening; If there are abnormalities, polysomnography → mechanical respiratory support (CPAP/BiPAP), not high-dose oxygen.
13. MCAS stabilization requires multi-pronged approach: H1+H2 blockers, mast cell stabilizers, low histamine diet, DAO substitution, trigger avoidance, pacing as MCAS therapy.
14. Do not reduce calcium (risk of osteoporosis); instead check magnesium status (whole blood analysis) - Magnesium is the cellular counterpart for uncontrolled calcium influx and protects against the ion-robbing chelation effect of fluoroquinolones on tissue (see III.D2).
15. Do not blindly supplement mito-boosters (Q10, PQQ, ALA) and polyphenol concentrates - they can trigger crashes if the respiratory chain is blocked, MCAS or glycolysis dependency. Only in the case of a laboratory-confirmed defect and under monitoring.
16. “Training through pain” is medically counterproductive when there is tendon/nerve involvement. Rest in the acute phase, then symptom-guided activity - not forced, not provoking pain.
17. Social invalidation ("don't act like that") measurably worsens outcomes. Inform relatives: FQAD is recognized by authorities, protection is medically justified, pressure is counterproductive.
18. When re-exercising after being confined to bed: Be aware of the risk of rhabdomyolysis. Warning signs (dark urine, rapid increase in pain, weakness) require immediate CK/creatinine control—emergency, not muscle soreness.
19. Critically examine commercial “stem cell/regenerative clinics” for FQAD: use the 7-question checklist (see Section VI). Missing study protocols, publications and safety data are warning signs - individual successes are no substitute for evidence.
20. Accelerated osteoarthritis in young FQAD sufferers is plausible (secondary to tendon/matrix damage + mechanical instability), but not proven as a direct FQ effect. Cartilage preservation when confined to bed: passive micro-mobilization (painless, without muscle strength) maintains synovial fluid circulation.
21. TIMPs (body's MMP brakes) require zinc as a cofactor; FQ also chelate zinc → check in the laboratory and compensate if there is a deficiency. Selenium is also relevant as a glutathione peroxidase cofactor.
22. Levofloxacin often heavier than ciprofloxacin: no liver back door, longer HWZ, perfected 3D key. Coffee sensitivity = CYP1A2 warning signal for Cipro risk; with Levo, kidney function (cystatin C, not just creatinine) is the bottleneck.
23. Be aware of cross-vulnerability: Severe reactions to mefloquine/chloroquine, chemotherapy drugs (topoisomerase inhibitors) or extreme codeine/SSRI sensitivity are clinical warning signs of increased risk of FQ toxicity.
24. Prioritized risk factors for FQ administration: (1) Mg²⁺ deficiency → (2) renal insufficiency/dehydration → (3) Zn²⁺ deficiency → (4) corticosteroids → (5) B12 deficiency. This ranking determines the sequence of stabilization measures.
25. Pre-medications as a precursor: PPI long-term therapy (Mg²⁺ depletion), statins (Q10 blockade + myotoxicity), bisphosphonates (enthesopathy), NSAIDs (GABA escalation), benzodiazepines (GABA receptor degradation). Medication history is mandatory before FQ prescription and for FQAD phenotyping.
26. FQAD is not “fluoride poisoning.” The fluorine atom on C6 is not split off in the body (<0.1%). It is the architectural enabler: increases lipophilicity (→ tissue penetration), protects against liver degradation (→ intact molecule persists) and sharpens the chelation claw at C3/C4.
27. Repeated FQ administration escalates the risk exponentially: cumulative ion trapping, irreversible mtDNA scars, depleted Mg²⁺/Zn²⁺ reserves, senescence accumulation. The first dose is often compensated; the repeated one is the last straw.
28. FQ residues in conventional meat (enrofloxacin → ciprofloxacin metabolite) can trigger relapses in those affected by FQAD. Particular danger: Bone broth from conventional farming (ion trapping in animal bones). Strictly avoid food triggers (glutamate, sugar, oxalates, histamine liberators) in the acute phase.
29. Complete damage mechanics: Front 0 (chelation disarms SOD/repair enzymes) → Front 1 (ETC blockade → Fenton → OH• radicals) → Front 2 (phototoxicity in skin only; only context of true fluoride release). Consequential damage: ~50% lipid peroxidation, ~45% protein oxidation, ~1–5% DNA damage (most serious long-term consequences).
30. Nemonoxacin (non-fluoroquinolone) as a pharmacological counterevidence: removal of the fluorine atom eliminates phototoxicity, reduces lipophilicity/ion trapping and accelerates liver degradation. Residual chelation risk remains (quinolone backbone), but catastrophic mito chain reaction is almost impossible.
31. Chelate affinity quantitatively proven: Antacids (Al/Mg) reduce FQ absorption by up to 90%, iron by 80-90%, zinc 50-70%, calcium 40-60%. The same force that blocks absorption in the intestine acts as an ion robber of enzymes and collagen after absorption in the tissue.
32. Supplementation paradox: Mg²⁺ and Zn²⁺ are the most important repair building blocks (priority 1), but iron is a radical amplifier in active oxidative stress (Fenton reaction) and calcium can increase excitotoxicity in NMDA overstimulation. Staggered protocol: first Mg/Zn, iron/Ca restrictively until stabilization.
33. AIFM1/IDH2-based functional diagnostics (research vision, 6 approaches): (1) IDH2 metabolite profiling, (2) Seahorse stress test, (3) ex vivo challenge with ciprofloxacin (potential gold standard: sample A/B comparison with membrane potential + respirometry + metabolomics), (4) circulating AIFM1 as Apoptosis markers, (5) Targeted NGS of binding pocket exons, (6) FLIM redox imaging. All approaches purely conceptual [2].
34. MCS (Multiple Chemical Sensitivity) in FQAD could result from the convergence of MCAS (immunological: unstable mast cells + DAMPs + Treg loss) and GABA filter failure (neurological: limbic misinterpretation of chemical stimuli). Dual therapy approach (MCAS stabilization + neural desensitization) could be considered, but without FQAD-specific evidence.
35. SOD2 and GST polymorphisms as additional genetic vulnerability factors: SOD2 (mitochondrial superoxide detoxification) and GSTM1/GSTT1 (phase II detoxification) determine the ability of the cell to cope with FQ-induced oxidative damage. Testable via standard pharmacogenetics panels.
36. Treg-mast cell collapse as an immunological causal chain for MCAS: FQ → butyrate deficiency + ROS → Treg depletion → mast cell disinhibition. Diagnostics: CD4⁺/CD25⁺/FoxP3⁺ by FACS (special diagnostics) + IL-10 + tryptase/methylhistamine. Decreased Tregs + increased mast cell markers = objectified immunological disinhibition.
37. Individual vulnerability is explained by seven converging levels: (1) AIFM1/IDH2 binding pocket (fit genetics), (2) cellular fire department (SOD2/GST polymorphisms), (3) magnesium reserve at the start of therapy, (4) liver clearance (CYP1A2 slow/fast metabolizer), (5) kidney function (eGFR/cystatin C, esp. at Levofloxacin), (6) pharmacological preload (PPI, statins, bisphosphonates, NSAIDs, benzodiazepines), (7) concurrent corticosteroids (officially recognized high-risk factor: double collagen attack + Mg/Ca depletion + immunosuppression).
38. Treg recovery as long-term MCAS rehabilitation: Five levers — (1) butyrate/postbiotics (HDAC inhibitor → FoxP3 → Treg differentiation, bypasses histamine trap), (2) vitamin D3+retinoic acid (Treg induction via VDR), (3) low-dose naltrexone/LDN (Th17→Treg shift), (4) Vagus nerve activation (cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway), (5) SPMs/resolvins from Omega-3 (inflammatory resolution + Treg recruitment). All 🟡 customized / not FQAD validated; "Low and Slow".
39. MCAS-safe butyrate increase via diet: Resistant starch (cooled potatoes/rice → histamine-free colon fermentation to butyrate), soluble fiber (apple pectin, psyllium), ghee as a direct source of butyrate, low-histamine probiotics (B. infantis, B. longum, L. rhamnosus GG). Strictly avoid: Fermented foods (yoghurt, kefir, sauerkraut) and histamine-producing strains (L. casei, L. bulgaricus).
40. Weight loss in FQAD is a warning signal, not a therapeutic goal. Catabolism → ammonia (brain fog) + sarcopenia + creatinine adulteration. Calories (≥2000 kcal) across three safe pillars: (1) fats as the main calorie carrier (ghee, coconut oil, olive oil - no lactate, no mast cell triggers), (2) complex carbohydrates (resistant starch), (3) high-quality protein (organic, FQ residue-free). Never reduce protein/carbohydrate - if you lose weight, increase fats.
41. FQAD/MCAS Food Classification (including fruit): 🟢 Safe (ghee, cooled potatoes/rice, broccoli/bok choy, pumpkin seeds/hemp seeds, organic frozen wild salmon, algae oil, macadamia, blueberries, applesauce, green bananas, cranberries, short meat broth). 🟡 Testing (eggs, quinoa/millet, cherries/peaches, watermelon, blackberries, psyllium, young cheese). 🔴 Avoid (oxalate robbers, histamine liberators including strawberries/citrus/ripe bananas, bone broth, dried fruit, sugar, glutamate).
42. Mineral water as a therapeutic tool: Still water with HCO₃⁻ >1000 mg/L (lactate buffer) + Mg >100 + Ca 250-500, but sulfate <50 (sulfur trap: sulfate-reducing bacteria → H₂S blocks complex IV like cyanide) and Na <20 (kidney protection). No carbonation (mechanical MCAS trigger). Avoid base powder with FQAD: carbonates = PPI effect, citrates = MCAS trigger (Aspergillus fermentation), baking soda = kidney Na⁺ flood.
43. Transdermal Mg intake (foot baths): Magnesium chloride flakes (no sulfate/Epsom), body temperature max. 36-37°C (MCAS temperature trap!), foot baths before a full bath, in the evening as a sleep aid (Mg = NMDA antagonist). In the case of a paradoxical weakness reaction due to Mg oil: dilute the brine (1:1), only the soles of the feet (not muscle areas), wash-off after 20 minutes. Three mechanisms: osmotic mast cell shock + Ca²⁺ channel blockade + vasodilation in dysautonomia.
44. Glycine paradox: Magnesium glycinate can trigger paradoxical fatigue in FQAD — not because of Mg, but because glycine, as an NMDA co-agonist, triggers excitotoxicity in FQ-blocked GABA receptors. MCAS-safe alternatives: Mg-malate (ATP support), Mg-taurate (GABAerg), transdermal MgCl₂. The reaction is qualitative (carrier intolerance), not quantitative (deficiency).
45. Metabolic PEM through food: In long-term FQAD/MCAS, histamine liberators (avocado), salicylates (peppers, onions) and gluten/lectins (spelled) trigger a cytokine cascade → Immune reaction deprives the weak mitochondria of remaining ATP → Exhaustion is not a lack of willpower, but a measurable loss of energy. Third trigger dimension besides histamine and oxalate: salicylate intolerance.
46. ​​Residual mitochondrial capacity can be calculated from PBMC laboratory values: (1) Efficiency quotient = ATP / mtDNA:ntDNA ratio → with compensatory mito proliferation + low ATP: single mitochondrion runs at <50%. (2) Rhodanase bottleneck: missing Fe-S clusters → aerobic capacity under load only ~5–6%. (3) PGC-1α block: no mito-neuogenesis → biogenesis trap. Standard laboratory findings often misinterpret this constellation as “efficient” — in FQAD it shows a massive energy deficit.
47. Gastroparesis in FQAD = vagus nerve block (dysautonomia) + MMP-weakened sphincter + MCAS mucosa. MCT oil (C8) as a gastroparesis bypass (no CCK reflex), slippery elm bark as mechanical mucous membrane protection, flash cooking (2–4 min.), zero tolerance for leftovers. No PPIs; no food in one piece - chop everything up to the maximum.
48. Growth hormones (HGH) and egg extracts (YTE, FGF) strictly contraindicated in FQAD: energy crash + senescence overdrive + MMP feeding + MCAS hormonal shock. Also avoid commercial drinking foods (Fresubin etc.) (maltodextrin/casein/carrageenan). For testosterone deficiency: bioidentical gel in microdose from an endocrinologist.
49. 311 nm UVB phototherapy as a source of vitamin D without oral Mg withdrawal (🟡/🔴 with reservations): micro-dosing (30-60 seconds/area), fixed distance (scalpel approach), rotating areas, MED titration, absolute eye protection requirement. Expectation: only ~50-100 IU/session — wanted with FQAD (crash-free). 311 nm is a factor of 2-3 more inefficient than 297 nm peak → a natural attenuator in MCAS. Risky - only after consultation with a doctor + vitamin D monitoring.
50. Complementary therapies for FQAD: Photobiomodulation (660/850 nm LED, no IR sauna, note biphasic dose), tVNS as the most logical device addition (vagus → gastroparesis), sound therapy (theta beats before meals, brown noise for tinnitus), Qigong as the best form of exercise for tendon problems, meditation before eating (CRH reduction → Mast cell stabilization). Avoid: alternating pressure mattresses (MCAS mechanostress), incense sticks (fine dust), isopathic Cipro (re-exposure).
51. Apply for nursing care level at FQAD: Informal application to nursing insurance fund. Care diary (2 weeks). FQAD-relevant modules: mobility (tendons), self-care (gastroparesis → special cuisine), disease management (therapy time expenditure), everyday life (brain fog). From level 1: relief amount 131 €/month. Without level of care: Section 38 SGB V (household help via family doctor certificate).
52. Quercetin as PGC-1α activator: Stimulates AMPK/SIRT1/PGC-1α → mitochondrial biogenesis (directly addresses PGC-1α blockade) + mast cell stabilization. Necessarily pure substance (no fillers), creep in microdosing (knife tip → 250-500 mg), as it is also a senolytic (risk of crash with full dose). Natural sources (capers, onions) not feasible for MCAS/gastroparesis; Buckwheat as the only compatible food source (23 mg/100g).
53. Omega-3 supplementation: TOTOX value necessarily <5 (oxidized fish oil = mito-toxin + histamine). Liquid algae oil (darkened glass bottle, cool immediately) instead of fish oil capsules. ALA from flaxseed is not enough for FQAD (conversion to EPA/DHA is massively restricted if mitochondria are damaged).
54. Regulatory consequence: ~83% of FQ regulations can be replaced by conventional AB + phages [63]. The ~17% previously considered “unreplaceable” (CAP, complicated UTI, prostatitis) can be addressed by nemonoxacin (non-fluoroquinolone, approved in Taiwan/China, phase III in the EU [69]): quinolone tissue permeability without phototoxicity, without ion trapping, with retained residual chelation risk. Triple combination of conventional AB + phage + nemonoxacin → almost complete FQ replacement possible in the future.
55. Retrograde signaling chain mtDNA → nDNA → collagen switch: FQ-damaged mtDNA → ROS + energy deficiency → retrograde signaling to cell nucleus → epigenetic shift (methylation/acetylation) at collagen genes in nDNA → tenocytes switch from elastic type I collagen to rigid type III collagen (scar tissue) around + MMP overproduction. The blueprint error is in the nDNA, but is a result of the mtDNA crisis. Collagen supplementation alone does not solve the problem - therapy must primarily reduce oxidative stress and activate PGC-1α so that the epigenetic switch resets.
56. Economic burden (own projection, three calculation paths disclosed): Path 1 bottom-up (damage categories summed) = €1.75 billion; Path 2 top-down (Kuula model [71], 4 million × €528) = €2.11 billion; Path 3 synthesis + long-term costs of chronic old cases =**3.5-4.5 billion €/year**. Biggest uncertainty factor: cumulative old cases (150,000–450,000) — cannot be reliably limited without an FQAD register. Prevalence in the order of Parkinson's (~420,000 [72]) and MS (~250,000 [73]).
57. Global dimension: Estimated ~40-50% of the world's population (~3.2-4 billion people) have been treated with FQ at least once (model estimate from DDD consumption data [74], [75], accumulated over ~40 years). With a conservative permanent damage rate of 0.1% → ~3.6 million potentially permanently injured people worldwide without registry data or ICD code.
58. 6-point reform plan for Germany: (1) No-fault damage fund based on the ONIAM model (France) — no-fault compensation. (2) Delayed prescribing (UK/NICE) — eliminates ~70-80% of minor prescriptions. (3) Real-time AI prescription check (Netherlands) — automatic cortisone/PPI/renal function alert. (4) Liability protection for non-prescription (Sweden) — eliminates defensive medicine pressure. (5) Risk classes on packaging + mandatory second opinion for outpatient FQ prescription. (6) National FQAD Registry (Denmark) — makes late damage visible and provides the evidence base for all other points.

## Conclusion

The responsible goal today is not the planning of a complete cure, but rather the systematic maximization of remission, organ recovery and stress reserve along defined endophenotypes and measurable organ endpoints. Deep remission up to extensive functional recovery remains an open possibility that can only be tested by research.
Why some sufferers recover and others become chronic is multifactorial and cannot be explained by a single factor. Dominant endophenotype, renal function, known risk factors (age, corticosteroids, diabetes), genetic vulnerability and secondary drivers of chronicity interact (see Section X.F). There is no reliable evidence of causal FQAD healing for any single supplement, diet or infusion therapy.

In the future, a sequential, biomarker-guided intervention program could theoretically enable extensive restoration of normal function for defined subgroups. The curative horizon (Section XIV) shows that biotechnological tools are fundamentally evolving—from genome editing to microbiome surgery. However, the development of such tools does not result in any proximity to clinical applicability in FQAD; each strategy must demonstrate safety, tissue-specific targeting, and clinical benefit prior to FQAD use.

The most important advance would not be a single new drug, but: FQAD biomarkers, FQAD subtypes, the first controlled FQAD intervention studies and an n-of-1 study culture that makes individualized hypotheses seriously testable — tied to the measurable endpoints of the recovery framework (Section XII).
